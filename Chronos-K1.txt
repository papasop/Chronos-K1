\begin{abstract}
\textit{Background.} Time has invariably entered physics as a primitive --- an underived input. The present paper proposes a structural framework in which the algebraic type of the local cost form is constrained by realizability axioms, with Lorentzian signature emerging as a consequence rather than an assumption.

\textit{Framework.} Here the cost of change is modelled through an information time $dt_{\mathrm{info}}:=d\Phi/H$. The object constrained is not $dt_{\mathrm{info}}$ itself, but the signed leading quadratic form $G$ associated with the local second-order expansion of $dt_{\mathrm{info}}^{2}$. Modelling this form as satisfying the realizability axioms (Axioms~R, E, T; see Appendix~\ref{app:realizability}) yields, under nondegeneracy, $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$, equivalently $\det G<0$; the unit-cost locus is $K(x)=x^{\top}Gx=1$.

\textit{Dynamics and thermodynamics.} Within the symplectic--dissipative class $GA=\alpha J-GD$, the evolution law takes the uniquely reduced form $\dot x=(J_G-D)\nabla V$. At critical damping the Law~III dynamics degenerates into a rank-one null flow with a conserved linear combination. This null-flow structure is universal for every $2\times2$ Lorentzian $G$. The stronger exact exponential recovery law, the pure $c^2$ rate, the $\Theta(c^{-2})$ recovery time, and the single frozen $c=0$ leaf are balanced-canonical special features rather than generic Lorentzian facts. Near $K=1$, the framework supplies the ingredients for a local Clausius relation, thereby providing the thermodynamic input to the Jacobson argument once the two external identifications $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ and $S\propto A$ are supplied.

\textit{Gravitational content.} The thermodynamic perspective above conditionally recovers the Einstein equations $R_{\mu\nu}-\tfrac12 R\,g_{\mu\nu}=8\pi T_{\mu\nu}$ via the Jacobson argument. Independently, in the spherical sector with $f>0$, the differential conditions $K_1=K_2=1$ for the cost-structural functionals $K_i:=\sigma_2^2\Box\ln\sigma_i$ are shown to be algebraically equivalent to the vacuum Einstein equations $R_{\mu\nu}=0$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:field}). The conditions $K_i=1$ are introduced at the field level as a candidate ansatz, motivated by but not derived from the point-level condition $K=1$ of Law~I; Theorem~\ref{thm:field} is therefore an algebraic reformulation of vacuum Einstein in cost-structural language, not a derivation from the framework axioms. Lorentzian signature is supplied internally at the point level throughout.

\textit{Path-integral extension.} Finally, we formulate a structural path-integral extension in which the ensemble variables are cost geometries $(G,\Phi,H)$, rather than trajectories $x(t)$ or unconstrained spacetime metrics. In a finite-dimensional minisuperspace model, the induced DeWitt-type density behaves as $\rho(G)\propto|\det G|^{-3/2}$, exposing a near-degenerate divergence at $\det G\to0$. A structural barrier $S_{\mathrm{barrier}}=\eta|\det G|^{-p}$, with $\eta,p>0$, renders the reduced measure finite and produces an interior non-degenerate saddle $u_{*}=(\eta p/\alpha)^{1/(p+1)}$. This is a measure-finite SCPI minisuperspace validator (\S\ref{sec:scpi}), not a construction of the full diffeomorphism-invariant gravitational path integral.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Lorentzian signature; Information time; Symplectic-dissipative dynamics; Jacobson argument; Rindler horizon; Structural path integral; DeWitt measure; Measure finiteness; Minisuperspace; Degenerate metric barrier

\section{Introduction: time as consequence}

We refer to the structural setup developed in this paper --- consisting of the cost-realizability bridge (Law~I), the symplectic-dissipative dynamics (Law~II), and the critical-damping fluctuation structure (Law~III) --- as the ``$K=1$ framework''.

\textit{The problem.} Riemann's 1854 Habilitation lecture observed that, for a continuous manifold, the basis of metric relations must be sought outside the manifold itself, in forces acting upon it~\cite{Riemann1854}.\footnote{Riemann's formulation: ``the basis of metric relations must be sought outside that actuality, in colligating forces that operate upon it.''} Most standard formulations of spacetime physics, however, assume a metric or a causal structure as input. The Jacobson argument~\cite{Jacobson} obtains the Einstein equations from thermodynamics on local Rindler horizons, but Lorentzian signature is universally assumed --- as in the approaches of Padmanabhan~\cite{Padmanabhan} and Verlinde~\cite{Verlinde}. The EPS axiomatisation~\cite{EPS} derives causal structure from free-fall and light propagation, but uses kinematic primitives. In each of these approaches to Riemann's open problem, either the signature is prescribed or the derivation presupposes kinematic primitives.

\textit{The gap.} In the ``local cost-based leading quadratic form'' sense adopted here, existing approaches typically take Lorentzian signature or an equivalent causal structure as input; the present work addresses the prior question: when does the cost structure itself enforce a Lorentzian leading symbol?

\textit{A local answer.} In the present modelling, time is neither a coordinate nor a metric primitive, but the cost layer associated with change. Information time is introduced as
\[
dt_{\mathrm{info}}:=\frac{d\Phi}{H},
\]
where $\Phi$ parametrises structural variation and $H>0$ is the local cost per unit variation. The object constrained in the present paper is not $dt_{\mathrm{info}}$ itself, but the signed leading quadratic form $G$ associated with the local second-order expansion of $dt_{\mathrm{info}}^{2}$, modelled as the leading form $Q$ of Appendix~\ref{app:realizability}. \textbf{The paper's first result is local and point-level: in a $2\times 2$ realizability setting, the cost structure forces Lorentzian type} --- Axioms~R, E, T together with nondegeneracy imply $\det G<0$, equivalently $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$ (\S\ref{sec:lawI}). What is obtained at this stage is the algebraic type of the leading form, not a full reconstruction of all metric data. The induced generator $J_G$, the threshold $d_c>0$, and the unit-cost locus $K(x)=x^{\top}Gx=1$ are then algebraic consequences of this sign structure; making $K=1$ a locally self-consistent locus requires the dynamical completion of Laws~II--III. \emph{Thus the present work adopts a structural rather than fully reconstructive framework: $\Phi$ and $H$ are not fixed to specific physical quantities, and the results below constrain the algebraic type of the leading quadratic form rather than derive all metric data from first principles.} Connecting this point-level result to the full Einstein equations requires, in the thermodynamic perspective, two external inputs: the identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ and the entropy--area law $S\propto A$. The further identification of the motivating information time $dt_{\mathrm{info}}$ with the later dynamical evolution parameter is not completed here and is left as an open problem.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Scope discipline.} Three logically distinct layers should not be conflated. (i) \emph{Point-level (Law~I):} the realizability axioms R, E, T force Lorentzian signature $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$ --- this is \textbf{derived}, but only fixes the algebraic type of the leading form, not all metric data. (ii) \emph{Thermodynamic route:} the Einstein equations are \textbf{conditionally recovered} via the Jacobson argument, only after the external identifications $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ and $S\propto A$. (iii) \emph{Field-level $K_i=1$:} the equivalence to vacuum Einstein is an \textbf{algebraic reformulation under a candidate ansatz}, motivated by but not derived from the point-level $K=1$ (Open Problem~1). The path-integral extension of \S\ref{sec:scpi} is a fourth, separate layer: a \textbf{finite-dimensional minisuperspace validator}, not a construction of the full gravitational path integral. Throughout, ``derived'' refers only to layer (i); layers (ii), (iii), and the path-integral extension are conditional or exploratory.

\medskip\noindent\textit{Two complementary perspectives on the Einstein equations, and a third statistical extension.}
\begin{itemize}

\medskip\noindent\textit{Main results.} \emph{Point-level}: under isotropic linearisation, Lorentzian signature, real spectrum of the induced generator $J_G$, and the positive stability threshold $d_{c}>0$ are equivalent (Theorem~\ref{thm:equivalence}). \emph{Thermodynamic perspective}: conditionally recovers $R_{\mu\nu}-\tfrac12 R\,g_{\mu\nu}=8\pi T_{\mu\nu}$ for general metrics, given the two external inputs $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ and $S\propto A$. \emph{Algebraic reformulation (\S~\ref{sec:field})}: in static, spherically symmetric metrics on intervals where $f>0$, the differential conditions $K_i=1$ for $i=1,2$ are algebraically equivalent to $R_{\mu\nu}=0$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:field}). \section{Metric from information time (Law~I)}\label{sec:lawI}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Law~I.} \textit{The metric $G$ is a non-degenerate symmetric bilinear form measuring local displacement cost, and the temporal direction is singled out as that of positive cost \textup{(Axiom~T of Appendix~\ref{app:realizability})}.}

\medskip\noindent\textit{Framework definition.} The present paper is organised around the following layers. \emph{Input layer}: a structural parameter $\Phi$ and a positive scalar cost $H$, defining the information time $dt_{\mathrm{info}}:=d\Phi/H$. \emph{Axiom layer}: a signed leading quadratic form $G$ associated with $dt_{\mathrm{info}}^{2}$, modelled as satisfying the realizability axioms (Axioms~R, E, T; see Appendix~\ref{app:realizability}) together with nondegeneracy. \emph{Additional condition}: the unit-cost locus $K(x):=x^{\top}Gx=1$, imposed as a point-level self-consistency condition. \emph{Dynamical completion}: Law~II gives the unique evolution $\dot x=(J_G-D)\nabla V$ within the symplectic--dissipative class $GA=\alpha J-GD$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:lawII}), and Law~III yields the restoring and fluctuation structure at critical damping (Theorem~\ref{thm:restoring}). \emph{Internal conclusions}: at the point level one obtains Lorentzian signature $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$, the threefold equivalence of Theorem~\ref{thm:equivalence}, and the local restoring law $\kappa_K=4d_c$ at a normalised eigenvector base point; conditionally, under the field-level ansatz $K_i=1$, one obtains algebraic equivalence to the Einstein vacuum equations in the spherical vacuum sector (Theorem~\ref{thm:field}). \emph{External inputs}: only for the thermodynamic perspective, the thermal identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ and the entropy--area law $S\propto A$ are required.

\medskip\noindent\textit{Main bridge.} The information time is defined by
\[
dt_{\mathrm{info}}:=\frac{d\Phi}{H},
\]
where $\Phi$ parametrises structural variation and $H>0$ is the local \emph{scalar} cost per unit variation of $\Phi$ (an entropic resistance, unrelated to the matrix weight $H_{*}$ of~\S\ref{sec:stability}). Associated with the local second-order part of $dt_{\mathrm{info}}^{2}$, we introduce a signed leading quadratic form $G$, heuristically written as $G:=\nabla^{2}(dt_{\mathrm{info}}^{2})$. Strictly speaking, this is shorthand: $G$ denotes the \emph{signed leading quadratic coefficient form} associated with $dt_{\mathrm{info}}^{2}$, not a full coordinate-level reconstruction obtained from an explicit expansion of $dt_{\mathrm{info}}$ in a specified chart. More precisely, the present framework models this signed leading coefficient form as the leading quadratic form $Q$ of Appendix~\ref{app:realizability}. Thus the step from $G$ to the realizability theorem is a \emph{structural identification at the level of the signed leading form}, not an independent re-derivation of Axioms~R, E, T from the information-time construction itself.

At the motivating level, the nonnegative cost is represented by the positive-part construction $(x^{\top}Gx)_{+}$, exactly as in the realizability framework of Appendix~\ref{app:realizability}. Once the signature conclusion has been obtained, the later algebra and dynamics work directly with the underlying signed leading form $G$ itself, and no longer require the positive-part construction. The positive-part cost belongs to the realizability layer that constrains the signature of the leading form. Once the Lorentzian signature has been fixed, the subsequent dynamical development does not call upon Axiom~R again. The later potential
\[
V=\tfrac12(K-1)^2
\]
is not the primitive cost functional of the realizability layer, but an additional dynamical choice introduced after the Lorentzian type of $G$ has been established, in order to study local restoring behaviour near $K=1$. Hence the Theorem~\ref{app:thm:lorentz} of Appendix~\ref{app:realizability} applies directly: Axioms~R, E, T together with nondegeneracy imply
\[
\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1),
\qquad\text{equivalently}\qquad
\det G<0.
\]
Axiom~R may be read intuitively as saying that there exist arbitrarily small forward displacements whose information cost vanishes relative to their size\footnote{Here ``size'' refers to the Euclidean norm $\|x\|$.}; this is the ingredient that rules out a positive-definite leading form. Thus, in the present modelling, the cost structure forces Lorentzian signature at the level of the signed leading form.

\medskip\noindent\textit{Deeper motivation.} Axioms~R, E, T of Appendix~\ref{app:realizability} may be viewed as a realizability encoding of the Hessian-level degeneracy structure associated with the local second-order cost form of $dt_{\mathrm{info}}^{2}$, motivated by the requirement that nontrivial variational motion remain realizable rather than collapse to trivial displacement. The present paper does not carry out a full reverse reconstruction of Axioms~R, E, T from that structure; rather, it uses the associated signed leading form $G$ together with the realizability theorem (Theorem~\ref{app:thm:lorentz}) of Appendix~\ref{app:realizability} as the operative bridge. For relative self-containment, \S\ref{sec:lawI} recalls only the minimal mechanism needed from Appendix~\ref{app:realizability}, rather than reproducing the full proof.

\medskip\noindent\textit{Scope of the claim.} What is constrained here is the \emph{algebraic type} of $G$ (its signature), not all of its specific values. Accordingly, $G$ should be read as a local cost-form forced by realizability, rather than as a complete reconstruction of the spacetime metric $g_{\mu\nu}$. The present work is therefore structural rather than fully reconstructive: $\Phi$ and $H$ are not fixed to specific physical quantities, and $dt_{\mathrm{info}}$ serves as the motivating cost-time layer from which the local leading form $G$ is introduced, rather than as a complete first-principles derivation of all metric data.

The quadratic form
\[
K(x):=x^{\top}Gx
\]
measures local temporal cost, and the condition $K=1$ identifies the self-consistent locus. We adopt the normalisation $K=1$ by a choice of cost units, without loss of generality at the level of the present local theory. This condition is \emph{not} a conclusion of Appendix~\ref{app:realizability}, but an additional self-consistency condition introduced in the present paper.

\medskip\noindent\textit{Role of information time in the present paper.} In the present work, $dt_{\mathrm{info}}$ plays a motivating and structural role: it specifies the cost-time layer from which the local leading form $G$ is introduced. The later dynamical sections use an effective evolution parameter $t$; an explicit first-principles identification between that parameter and $dt_{\mathrm{info}}$ is not derived here. Throughout this paper, the dynamical evolution parameter $t$ is an effective evolution time independent of $dt_{\mathrm{info}}$; establishing their relation is left to future work. In this sense, Law~I establishes the local cost-geometric layer of the framework, while a full dynamical reconstruction from information time alone is left to future work. Throughout the paper, this $2\times 2$ construction is applied on distinguished local two-planes, such as the $(t,r)$ wedge of a local Rindler horizon in the thermodynamic perspective and the radial-time plane in spherical symmetry for the algebraic reformulation. These are not arbitrary two-planes: they are the physically distinguished local planes selected by the problem under consideration.

\medskip\noindent\textit{Sign conventions.} In this section, $G$ is written in the cost-sign convention, so that the temporal direction carries positive cost. In later sections, especially in the Rindler realisation of the thermodynamic perspective, we switch to the spacetime-sign convention
\[
g_{\mu\nu}=-G_{\mu\nu}.
\]
This overall sign reversal does not affect the Lorentzian signature statement $\det G<0$; it changes only the convention used when the quadratic form is presented in spacetime language. In later sections, when the physical spacetime metric is used directly, we switch notation explicitly to $g_{\mu\nu}$ to avoid conflating it with the cost-sign form of \S\ref{sec:lawI}.

\medskip\noindent\textit{Minimal content used from Appendix~\ref{app:realizability}.} For relative self-containment, the mechanism used here is the following. Axiom~E reduces the leading-order displacement cost to a non-degenerate quadratic type $Q$. Axiom~T supplies a distinguished temporal direction with strictly positive cost, thereby excluding a purely negative-definite leading form. Axiom~R, via the limiting argument proved in Appendix~\ref{app:realizability}, requires spatially nontrivial forward displacements whose cost-to-size ratio tends to zero, thereby excluding a positive-definite leading form. In two dimensions, once both definite signatures are excluded and nondegeneracy is imposed, the only remaining inertia is $(1,1)$. Identifying the present $G$ with this signed leading form $Q$, one obtains $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$, equivalently $\det G<0$. The full statement, including the precise cost functional and the limiting argument in Axiom~R, is proved in Appendix~\ref{app:realizability}.

\medskip\noindent\textit{Consequence.} Lorentzian signature is therefore derived from the cost structure at the level of the leading form: $G$ is indefinite, and its null set
\[
\{x:x^{\top}Gx=0\}
\]
determines a local causal structure. In general relativity, the Lorentzian metric is prescribed on a pre-existing differentiable manifold; here the Lorentzian algebraic type of the leading cost form is derived rather than postulated.

\section{Symplectic structure from \texorpdfstring{$G$}{G}}\label{sec:symplectic}

\noindent\textit{From geometry to dynamics.} Law~I specifies the algebraic type of $G$, but not how the system evolves. The next step is to obtain from $G$, together with a conservative compatibility condition and a Euclidean normalisation convention, a canonical generator usable for Laws~II--III and stability analysis. The conservative compatibility condition is that the free drift preserve the cost structure encoded by $K(x)=x^{\top}Gx$; at the linear level, this requires $GM$ to be skew-symmetric. The construction below (Theorem~\ref{thm:unique}) requires only nondegeneracy; the Lorentzian reading --- spectral reality and the stability threshold $d_c>0$ --- enters subsequently, through Theorem~\ref{thm:spectral} and the isotropic interpretation of \S\ref{sec:stability}.

\medskip

\begin{theorem}[Structural Uniqueness]\label{thm:unique}
Let $G$ be a non-degenerate symmetric $2\times 2$ real matrix. Among all matrices $M$ such that
\textup{(i)}~$GM$ is skew-symmetric and
\textup{(ii)}~$M=\alpha J$ when $G=I$ \textup{(Euclidean normalisation)},
the unique solution is $J_G=\alpha G^{-1}J$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Since $\dim(\mathrm{Skew}_2)=1$ and $J$ spans $\mathrm{Skew}_2$, condition~\textup{(i)} forces $GM=\beta J$ for some $\beta\in\mathbb{R}$, hence $M=\beta G^{-1}J$; condition~\textup{(ii)} fixes $\beta=\alpha$.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Non-degeneracy vs.\ signature]
Theorem~\ref{thm:unique} requires only $\det G\neq 0$; it holds for any signature. Theorem~\ref{thm:spectral} provides the spectral computation; the Lorentzian condition becomes a stability criterion in Theorem~\ref{thm:equivalence}, under isotropic linearisation. Since $GJ_G=\alpha J$ is skew-symmetric, $J_G$ is $G$-antisymmetric by construction; the Lorentzian condition forces $J_G$ to be a hyperbolic element with real eigenvalues, yielding $d_c>0$.
\end{remark}

\begin{theorem}[Spectral Reality]\label{thm:spectral}
Let $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$. The characteristic polynomial of $G^{-1}J$ is $\lambda^2+1/\det G=0$. Since $\det G<0$, the roots are real: $\lambda=\pm\sqrt{-1/\det G}$. In the isotropic reading, the corresponding stability boundary is
\begin{equation}
d_c(\alpha)=\alpha\sqrt{-1/\det G}>0.\label{eq:dc}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$\mathrm{tr}(G^{-1}J)=0$ and $\det(G^{-1}J)=1/\det G$; the characteristic polynomial $\lambda^2+1/\det G=0$ then gives $\mathrm{spec}(J_G)=\{\pm d_c\}$. For the isotropic case $H_*=I$, $\max \mathrm{Re}\,\mathrm{spec}(J_G-dI)=d_c-d$, which vanishes at $d=d_c$.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Necessity of Lorentzian Signature]\label{thm:necessity}
If $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(2,0)$ or $(0,2)$, then $\det G>0$, so $\lambda^2+1/\det G=0$ has purely imaginary roots; no positive real $d_c>0$ exists in the sense of Theorem~\ref{thm:equivalence}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$\det G>0$ gives $1/\det G>0$, so $\lambda^2=-1/\det G<0$ and both roots are purely imaginary; no positive real eigenvalue $d_c>0$ exists.\qed
\end{proof}

\section{The isotropic stability boundary}\label{sec:stability}

\noindent\textit{From algebraic identity to dynamical threshold.}
Section~\ref{sec:symplectic} established the purely algebraic identity
$d_{c}=\alpha\sqrt{-1/\det G}$ for any $2\times2$ Lorentzian $G$:
$d_{c}$ is simply the magnitude of the real eigenvalues of $J_{G}$.
No dynamics has been invoked so far.
The purpose of the present section is to give this algebraic quantity
a \emph{stability interpretation} under one additional specification:
the linearisation weight matrix is taken as $H_{*}=I$ (isotropic).
When $H_{*}=I$, the auxiliary linear system $d(\delta x)/dt=(J_{G}-dI)\delta x$
has $\max\mathrm{Re}\,\mathrm{spec}(J_{G}-dI)=d_{c}-d$; hence $d>d_{c}$
guarantees exponential decay of all modes, $d=d_{c}$ is critical damping,
and $d_{c}>0$ becomes a positive stability threshold.
This interprets the signature--spectrum triple of Fig.~\ref{fig:triangle}
in dynamical terms.  For general positive-definite $H_{*}\neq I$,
the zero-eigenvalue locus of $(J_{G}-dI)H_{*}$ is unchanged
(Proposition~\ref{prop:diag_inv}), but the full Lyapunov stability
threshold may shift (Remark~\ref{rem:Hstar_dep}); the equivalence
stated in Theorem~\ref{thm:equivalence} therefore holds \emph{within
the isotropic reading}.  No new algebraic derivation is introduced
in this section.

\medskip

\begin{theorem}[Signature-Stability Equivalence]\label{thm:equivalence}
For non-degenerate symmetric $2\times 2$ real $G$, under the isotropic linearisation $H_{*}=I$, the following are equivalent:
\begin{itemize}
\item[\textup{(a)}] The isotropic stability threshold
\[
d_{c}=\alpha\sqrt{-1/\det G}
\]
is a positive real quantity.
\item[\textup{(b)}] $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$ \textup{(Lorentzian)}, equivalently $\det G<0$.
\end{itemize}
For general $H_{*}$, the zero-eigenvalue locus in $d$ is invariant under invertible right multiplication by $H_{*}$ (Proposition~\ref{prop:diag_inv}), but the full real-part stability interpretation may fail (Remark~\ref{rem:Hstar_dep}); thus $d_{c}$ is a signature invariant only within the isotropic stability interpretation.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$d_{c}>0 \Leftrightarrow -1/\det G>0$ (eq.~\eqref{eq:dc}) $\Leftrightarrow \det G<0 \Leftrightarrow \mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$, via Theorems~\ref{thm:spectral} and~\ref{thm:necessity}.\qed
\end{proof}

\noindent The signature--spectrum--stability triple correspondence established by Theorems~\ref{thm:spectral}--\ref{thm:equivalence} is summarised in Fig.~\ref{fig:triangle}.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Scope.} The closed-form $d_{c}=\alpha\sqrt{-1/\det G}$ relies on $\dim(\mathrm{Skew}_{2})=1$; higher-dimensional extensions require additional structure and are not fully developed in the present paper.

\begin{remark}[Isotropic scaling]
$H$ (scalar, \S\ref{sec:lawI}) is the local cost per unit variation of $\Phi$ (an entropic resistance), appearing in $dt_{\mathrm{info}}=d\Phi/H$; $H_{*}$ (matrix) denotes the cost-function weight matrix in $d(\delta x)/dt=(J_{G}-dI)H_{*}\,\delta x$ (in the linearised stability analysis of the present section). The two are distinct mathematical objects and are not identified in the present paper; establishing an explicit relation between them from a single deeper cost principle is left to future work. $H_{*}=I$ is the default. For $H_{*}=cI$ ($c>0$), the zero-real-part threshold in $d$ remains $d_{c}$, although rates are rescaled by $c$. For general positive-definite $H_{*}$, the full stability threshold may shift.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Dependence on $H_{*}$]\label{rem:Hstar_dep}
With $G=\mathrm{diag}(0.1,-10)$ and $H_{*}=\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}1&0.9\\0.9&1\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$, $d_{c}^{\mathrm{iso}}=1$ while $d_{c}(H_{*})=4.55$, confirming that $d_{c}$ may depend on $H_{*}$ outside the isotropic class. Here $d_{c}(H_{*})$ refers to the full real-part stability threshold, not the zero-eigenvalue locus (which remains at $d_{c}=1$ by Proposition~\ref{prop:diag_inv}).
\end{remark}

\begin{proposition}[$H_{*}$-invariance of the zero-eigenvalue locus]\label{prop:diag_inv}
For any invertible $H_{*}$, the zero-eigenvalue values of $(J_{G}-dI)H_{*}$ coincide with those of $J_{G}-dI$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
$\det((J_{G}-dI)H_{*})=\det(J_{G}-dI)\det H_{*}$; since $\det H_{*}\neq 0$, the determinant vanishes exactly when $\det(J_{G}-dI)=0$.\qed
\end{proof}
\begin{remark}
For the Rindler metric with diagonal $H_{*}>0$, the zero-eigenvalue threshold in $d$ remains $d_{c}=\alpha/(\kappa\ell)$. The thermodynamic applications of \S~\ref{sec:thermo} use this threshold directly, without requiring the full isotropic equivalence of Theorem~\ref{thm:equivalence}.
\end{remark}

\section{Unique dynamics (Law~II)}\label{sec:lawII}

\noindent\textbf{Law~II.} \textit{The generator of the dynamics splits as $GA = \alpha J - GD$ with $D\ge 0$ (positive-semidefinite dissipation). This symplectic-dissipative decomposition is the content of Law~II; it is a structural axiom, not derived from Laws~I or~III, motivated by the universal Helmholtz-type splitting of physical evolution into reversible (symplectic) and irreversible (dissipative) parts~\cite{GENERIC}. Under this axiom, the generator is uniquely reduced to the form $J_{G}-D$, so that the evolution law for any smooth potential $V$ is
\[
\dot{x}=(J_{G}-D)\nabla V,
\]
with the dissipation matrix $D$ remaining free within the positive-semidefinite class until it is fixed further, in the isotropic critical-damping reading, by Law~III. The local restoring role of $\{K=1\}$ is specified separately by Law~III.}

The splitting $GA=\alpha J-GD$ is the axiomatic content of Law~II. Combined with Theorem~\ref{thm:unique} (which fixes $J_{G}$), this yields the unique admissible evolution law (Theorem~\ref{thm:lawII}).

\begin{theorem}[Canonical Form of Law~II]\label{thm:lawII}
Let $G$ be non-degenerate and let $J_{G}=\alpha G^{-1}J$ \textup{(Theorem~\ref{thm:unique})}. For any generator satisfying the Law~II constraint
\[
GA = \alpha J - GD,\qquad D \ge 0,
\]
the evolution law for any smooth potential $V$ takes the uniquely reduced form
\[
\dot{x} = (J_{G}-D)\,\nabla V.
\]
Thus the physically admissible evolution laws in the present framework are exactly those of the form $(J_G-D)\nabla V$ with $D\ge 0$, in the spirit of the Helmholtz/GENERIC splitting~\cite{GENERIC}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$A=G^{-1}(\alpha J-GD)=J_{G}-D$.\qed
\end{proof}

For Lorentzian $G$, $J_G$ has symmetric part $S=(J_{G}+J_{G}^{\top})/2\neq 0$, departing from the GENERIC requirement of Euclidean antisymmetry.

\section{Critical damping and fluctuation structure (Law~III)}\label{sec:lawIII}

\noindent\textbf{Law~III (Dissipation selection).}
\emph{The dissipation matrix $D$ is selected by requiring that
$K{=}1$ be a locally stable equilibrium of the drift
$\dot x=(J_G-D)\nabla V$ with $V=\tfrac12(K{-}1)^2$, and by choosing
the smallest isotropic dissipation compatible with that stability.}

\medskip

\noindent\textit{From open dynamics to a concrete physical model.}
Law~II fixes the algebraic form of the generator,
$\dot x=(J_G-D)\nabla V$ with $D\ge 0$, but leaves $D$ unspecified.
To obtain a concrete physical model, one must therefore select a
particular dissipation matrix.
The natural criterion is that the unit-cost locus $K{=}1$, which
plays the role of a self-consistency condition in the framework,
should be dynamically stable: small departures from $K{=}1$ should be
driven back toward it.
Among such stabilising choices, we select the one with the smallest
isotropic dissipation.

Restricting to the isotropic class $D=dI$ ($d>0$), the strength of the
normal restoring behaviour is controlled by the dissipation level $d$,
while the relevant stability scale is set by the spectral quantity
$d_{c}$ introduced in \S\ref{sec:stability}, namely the magnitude of the
real eigenvalues of the hyperbolic symplectic generator $J_{G}$.
Accordingly, stability requires $d\ge d_{c}$, and the boundary choice
$d=d_{c}$ gives critical damping.
It is therefore the unique member of the isotropic family that
stabilises $K{=}1$ with minimal dissipation.
Thus Law~III selects
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Dcritical}
D = d_{c}I,
\end{equation}
with
\[
d_{c}=\alpha\sqrt{-1/\det G},
\]
as obtained in \S\ref{sec:symplectic}.

The remainder of this section develops the consequences of this
selection: first, the local effective restoring rate toward $K{=}1$
(Theorem~\ref{thm:restoring}); and second, the natural separation of
fluctuations on $\{K=1\}$ into a thermodynamic class and a purely
kinematic class.

\medskip

\begin{theorem}[Local Effective Restoring Rate]\label{thm:restoring}
Let $G$ be a $2\times 2$ Lorentzian metric, $D=d_{c}I$ (Law~III), and $J_{G}=\alpha G^{-1}J$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:unique}). Define $K(x)=x^{\top}Gx$, $V=\tfrac{1}{2}(K-1)^{2}$, with drift $\dot{x}=(J_{G}-d_{c}I)\nabla V$. Then for all $x\in\mathbb{R}^{2}$:
\begin{equation}
\dot{K} = 4(K-1)\bigl[\alpha\,x^{\top}(JG)\,x - d_{c}\,x^{\top}G^{2}x\bigr].\label{eq:Kdot}
\end{equation}
At any $x_{*}\in\{K=1\}$ with $Gx_{*}=\lambda x_{*}$, the term $x_{*}^{\top}JGx_{*}=0$; freezing the coefficient in~\eqref{eq:Kdot} at such an $x_{*}$ and retaining the first-order term in $(K-1)$ gives
\begin{equation}
\dot{K} = -4\,d_{c}\,x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}\cdot(K-1)+o(K-1).\label{eq:Kdot_eigen}
\end{equation}
If, in addition, the base point is normalised by $x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}=1$ (i.e., $x_*$ is a unit-eigenvalue $G$-eigenvector), then
\begin{equation}
\dot{K} = -\kappa_{K}(K-1)+o(K-1),\qquad \kappa_{K}=4\,d_{c}.\label{eq:kappa}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$\dot{K}=2x^{\top}G\dot{x}=4(K-1)\,x^{\top}G(J_{G}-d_{c}I)Gx$. Since $GJ_{G}=\alpha J$, right-multiplying by $G$ gives $GJ_{G}G=\alpha(JG)$, yielding~\eqref{eq:Kdot}. At a $G$-eigenvector, $x_{*}^{\top}JGx_{*}=\lambda\,x_{*}^{\top}Jx_{*}=0$ by skew-symmetry.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Base-point dependence]
The local effective restoring rate is $\kappa_{K}(x_{*})=4d_{c}\cdot x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}$; it depends on the base point. For the Rindler metric, $x_{*}=(0,1)$ is the unique $G$-eigenvector on $\{K{=}1\}$ with $x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}=1$. The factor~$4=2\times 2$ reflects two sources: the quadratic form~$K$ and the quadratic penalty~$V$; each contributes a factor of~$2$ upon differentiation.
\end{remark}

\subsection*{Two classes of fluctuation on $\{K=1\}$}

Adding isotropic additive white noise (It\^{o} convention) to Law~II gives $dx=(J_{G}-D)\nabla V\,dt+\sigma\,dW_{t}$, where $W_{t}$ is a standard $\mathbb{R}^{2}$-valued Wiener process and $\sigma>0$ is constant. At $D=d_{c}I$ (critical damping, Law~III), two geometrically distinct classes arise:

\medskip\noindent
\textit{Class~A} (thermodynamic): fluctuations of $K$ perpendicular to $\{K=1\}$ --- departures from metric self-consistency --- governed by restoring rate $\kappa_{K}(x_{*})=4d_{c}\,x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}$; the linearised stationary distribution of the $K$-fluctuation at a fixed base point is Gaussian with variance $\sigma^{2}/(2d_{c})$, as follows from the standard stationary distribution of the one-dimensional OU process~\cite{Gardiner1985}. Although both the restoring rate and the $K$-space noise amplitude depend on $x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}$, this factor cancels in the OU variance.

\medskip\noindent
\textit{Class~B} (kinematic; thermodynamically inert here): the tangential component of the fluctuation along $\{K=1\}$ in the boost direction. For the Rindler metric $g=\mathrm{diag}(-(\kappa\ell)^{2},1)$, $\{K=1\}$ is the hyperbola $-(\kappa\ell)^{2}x_{1}^{2}+x_{2}^{2}=1$; motion along it generates Lorentz boosts, changing the inertial observer but not the thermodynamic state. The Hessian of $V$ restricted to $\{K=1\}$ is rank-one with null space containing this tangent, confirming that Class~B carries no restoring force and no heat. The geometric picture of the two classes on the $\{K=1\}$ hyperbola is shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:K1geometry}.

\noindent
\textbf{Summary of logical status.}
Law~III closes the dynamical sector by selecting $D=d_{c}I$ through a
stability criterion with minimal isotropic dissipation.
With this choice, Theorem~\ref{thm:restoring} shows that $K{=}1$ is a
locally restoring equilibrium, while the fluctuation analysis separates
the dynamics into a thermodynamic channel (Class~A) and a purely
kinematic channel (Class~B).
The thermodynamic channel will supply the local Clausius relation in
the next section; the kinematic channel carries no heat and is inert
for that argument.

\subsection{Critical damping as null-flow selection}\label{sec:nullflow}

The critical-damping selection $D=d_cI$ of Law~III has a sharper
structural consequence than minimal stabilisation alone.  Write
\[
        A_c:=J_G-d_cI,
        \qquad
        J_G=\alpha G^{-1}J,
        \qquad
        d_c=\alpha\sqrt{-1/\det G}.
\]
For the canonical Lorentzian form $G=\operatorname{diag}(1,-1)$ with
$\alpha=1$, one has
\[
        A_c=
        \begin{pmatrix}
        -1&1\\[2pt]
        1&-1
        \end{pmatrix},
        \qquad
        \operatorname{im}(A_c)=\operatorname{span}\{(-1,1)\},
        \qquad
        (-1,1)^TG(-1,1)=0 .
\]
Thus the canonical critical drift is already a rank-one null flow; the
full balanced-canonical leaf analysis is given in
Corollary~\ref{prop:recovery}.  The next proposition records the part of
this structure that survives for every $2\times2$ Lorentzian $G$.

\begin{proposition}[Universal critical null-flow]
\label{prop:nullflow-universal}
Let $G$ be any $2\times2$ Lorentzian metric, $\det G<0$.  Define
\[
        J_G=\alpha G^{-1}J,
        \qquad
        d_c=\alpha\sqrt{-1/\det G},
        \qquad
        A_c=J_G-d_cI .
\]
Then
\[
        \det A_c=0,
        \qquad
        \operatorname{spec}(A_c)=\{0,-2d_c\},
        \qquad
        \operatorname{rank}(A_c)=1 .
\]
Consequently:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[\textup{(i)}]
      the left null covector $\ell$ of $A_c$ defines a conserved
      linear charge
      \[
            c_\ell:=\ell^Tx ,
      \]
      unique up to nonzero normalization, for the critically damped drift
      \[
            \dot x=2(K-1)A_cGx,
            \qquad
            K=x^TGx .
      \]
      Indeed,
      \[
            \ell^TA_c=0
            \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
            \frac{d}{dt}(\ell^Tx)
            =
            2(K-1)\ell^TA_cGx
            =
            0 .
      \]

\item[\textup{(ii)}]
      the drift is confined to a single $G$-null ray
      \[
            u\in\operatorname{im}(A_c),
            \qquad
            u^TGu=0,
      \]
      while the kernel is the other $G$-null ray
      \[
            v\in\ker(A_c),
            \qquad
            v^TGv=0 .
      \]
      The two rays are distinct.
\end{enumerate}
Thus critical damping universally selects a rank-one null flow on one
$G$-null ray, with the kernel on the other, and a conserved linear charge
unique up to normalization, for every Lorentzian $G$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The operator $J_G$ is traceless and satisfies
\[
        \det J_G
        =
        \alpha^2\det(G^{-1})\det J
        =
        \frac{\alpha^2}{\det G}
        <0 .
\]
Hence
\[
        \operatorname{spec}(J_G)=\{+d_c,-d_c\},
        \qquad
        d_c=\alpha\sqrt{-1/\det G}.
\]
Therefore
\[
        \operatorname{spec}(A_c)
        =
        \operatorname{spec}(J_G-d_cI)
        =
        \{0,-2d_c\}.
\]
This gives
\[
        \det A_c=0,
        \qquad
        \operatorname{tr}A_c=-2d_c\neq0,
        \qquad
        \operatorname{rank}(A_c)=1 .
\]

For the conserved charge, if $\ell^TA_c=0$, then along
$\dot x=2(K-1)A_cGx$,
\[
        \frac{d}{dt}(\ell^Tx)
        =
        \ell^T\dot x
        =
        2(K-1)\ell^TA_cGx
        =
        0 .
\]

It remains to identify the null rays.  Since
\[
        GJ_G=\alpha J
\]
is skew-symmetric, any real eigenvector $w$ of $J_G$ with nonzero
eigenvalue $\lambda$ satisfies
\[
        0
        =
        w^T(\alpha J)w
        =
        w^TGJ_Gw
        =
        \lambda\,w^TGw .
\]
Thus $w^TGw=0$.  The two eigenvalues of $J_G$ are $+d_c$ and
$-d_c$, with two distinct real eigendirections.  Shifting by $-d_cI$
makes the $+d_c$ eigendirection the kernel of $A_c$, while the
remaining eigendirection spans the image of the rank-one map $A_c$.
These are precisely the two distinct $G$-null rays.
\end{proof}

The companion scripts \texttt{op6\_symbolic.py} and
\texttt{op6\_nullflow\_general.py} reproduce the identities of
Proposition~\ref{prop:nullflow-universal} symbolically and numerically.

We use three related charge notations in this subsection.  The symbol
$c_\ell=\ell^Tx$ denotes the conserved charge for a general Lorentzian
$G$, determined only up to nonzero normalization.  In the normalized
balanced-canonical gauge $G=\operatorname{diag}(1,-1)$, we choose the
unnormalized charge $c=x_1+x_2$.  The unit-normalized version is
\[
        \hat c=\frac{x_1+x_2}{\sqrt2}.
\]
The recovery Corollary is stated in terms of $c$, while the generic rate
law is stated in terms of $\hat c$.

\begin{corollary}[Balanced-canonical recovery law]
\label{prop:recovery}
Specialize Proposition~\ref{prop:nullflow-universal} to the normalized
balanced-canonical gauge
\[
        G=\operatorname{diag}(1,-1),
\]
for which the conserved charge may be taken as
\[
        c=x_1+x_2 .
\]
For $\alpha=1$, one has $d_c=1$, $J_G=G^{-1}J$, and
\[
        A_c
        =
        \begin{pmatrix}
        -1&1\\[2pt]
        1&-1
        \end{pmatrix},
        \qquad
        \det A_c=0,
        \qquad
        \operatorname{rank}(A_c)=1 .
\]
Moreover,
\[
        \operatorname{im}(A_c)=\operatorname{span}\{(-1,1)\},
        \qquad
        (-1,1)^TG(-1,1)=0 .
\]
With
\[
        V=\frac12(K-1)^2,
        \qquad
        K=x^TGx,
        \qquad
        \nabla V=2(K-1)Gx,
\]
the critically damped drift is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:nullflow}
        \dot x
        =
        A_c\nabla V
        =
        2(K-1)(x_1+x_2)(-1,1).
\end{equation}
The charge $c=x_1+x_2$ is an exact first integral, since
\[
        \frac{d}{dt}(x_1+x_2)
        =
        (1,1)\cdot\dot x
        =
        2(K-1)(x_1+x_2)\bigl[(1,1)\cdot(-1,1)\bigr]
        =
        0 .
\]
Thus $c$ foliates $\mathbb R^2$ into invariant leaves
$\{x_1+x_2=c\}$.

On each invariant leaf, the image direction of $A_c$ is $G$-null by
Proposition~\ref{prop:nullflow-universal}.  Hence $K$ is exactly affine
along the leaf, and the induced $(K-1)$-equation is exactly linear, not
merely a linearization near $\{K=1\}$:
\[
        \frac{d}{dt}(K-1)
        =
        -4c^2(K-1),
\]
and therefore
\[
        K(t)-1
        =
        (K_0-1)e^{-4c^2t}
\]
exactly on the leaf.  Consequently, for any tolerance
\[
        \varepsilon\in(0,|K_0-1|),
\]
the recovery time is
\[
        T_{\mathrm{recover}}(c)
        =
        \frac{1}{4c^2}
        \log\frac{|K_0-1|}{\varepsilon}
        =
        \Theta(c^{-2})
        \qquad
        (c\to0).
\]

The drift~\eqref{eq:nullflow} vanishes on two distinct loci: the target
$\{K=1\}$, through the factor $K-1$, and the freeze line
\[
        \Sigma_0:=\{x\in\mathbb R^2:x_1+x_2=0\},
\]
through the factor $x_1+x_2$.  On $\Sigma_0$, one has $x_2=-x_1$ and
therefore
\[
        K|_{\Sigma_0}=0 .
\]
Hence $\Sigma_0$ lies entirely off the target $\{K=1\}$ and the drift
freezes there, with no restoring force.  For $c\neq0$, the convergence is
exponential but not finite-time:
\[
        K(t)\to1
        \qquad
        \text{only as}
        \qquad
        t\to\infty .
\]
In this balanced-canonical case, the freeze locus $\Sigma_0$ coincides
with the degenerate leaf $\{c=0\}$, convergence holds on
\[
        \mathbb R^2\setminus\Sigma_0,
\]
and $\{K=1\}$ is attracting on every $c\neq0$ leaf.

For an overall balanced-canonical rescaling
\[
        G=s\,\operatorname{diag}(1,-1),
        \qquad
        s>0,
\]
the same mechanism gives
\[
        \frac{d}{dt}(K-1)
        =
        -4s\,c^2(K-1),
\]
so the recovery remains exactly exponential and still satisfies
\[
        T_{\mathrm{recover}}(c)=\Theta(c^{-2}),
\]
with only the prefactor changed by the overall scale.

Equivalently, using the unit-normalized charge
\[
        \hat c=\frac{x_1+x_2}{\sqrt2},
\]
the balanced-canonical rate law reads
\[
        \frac{d}{dt}(K-1)
        =
        -\kappa \hat c^2(K-1),
        \qquad
        \kappa=8s .
\]
For the normalized case $s=1$, this is consistent with
\[
        4(x_1+x_2)^2
        =
        8\hat c^2 .
\]

Thus the exact exponential law, pure $c^2$ rate,
$\Theta(c^{-2})$ recovery, frozen $c=0$ leaf, and the coincidence
\[
        \Sigma_0=\{c=0\}
\]
are balanced-canonical features.  Their generic Lorentzian status is given
in Remark~\ref{rem:generic-split}.
\end{corollary}

\begin{remark}[Canonical numerical diagnostic]
Integration of the canonical critically damped system confirms the
structure above.  In random initial-condition tests, non-convergent
trajectories concentrate sharply near $\Sigma_0$: sampled initial points
within Euclidean distance of order $10^{-1}$ of $\Sigma_0$ fail to reach
$K=1$ within a finite integration horizon, consistent with the rate tending
to zero as $c=x_1+x_2\to0$.  Points well separated from $\Sigma_0$
converge.  The critically damped velocity field is moreover null
throughout, since its image is $\operatorname{span}\{(-1,1)\}$.  This
supports reading $\Sigma_0$ as a genuine geometric degeneracy locus of
Law~III rather than a numerical artifact.  The general Lorentzian extension
is given in Proposition~\ref{prop:nullflow-universal} and
Remark~\ref{rem:generic-split}.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Exact-leaf numerical reproduction of the balanced-canonical law]
Direct integration on exact invariant leaves reproduces the closed-form
recovery law of Corollary~\ref{prop:recovery} to solver accuracy.  With
exact on-leaf initialisation, so that $x_1+x_2=c$ is held exactly, the
measured recovery time matches
\[
        T_{\mathrm{recover}}
        =
        \frac{1}{4c^2}
        \log\frac{|K_0-1|}{\varepsilon}
\]
pointwise, and a log--log fit of recovery time against $c$ gives
\[
        \mathrm{slope}=-2.000,
        \qquad
        R^2=1.000,
\]
in agreement with
\[
        T_{\mathrm{recover}}=\Theta(c^{-2})
\]
up to the logarithmic factor
\[
        \log\frac{|K_0-1|}{\varepsilon}.
\]
The rescaled product
\[
        T_{\mathrm{recover}}\,c^2
        =
        \frac14
        \log\frac{|K_0-1|}{\varepsilon}
\]
is therefore constant along leaves at fixed $(K_0,\varepsilon)$.
Deviations from the $c^{-2}$ exponent observed in earlier control runs
arose from finite-window identifiability effects: curves near the freeze
leaf $\Sigma_0$ decay too slowly for the rate to be statistically resolved
within a finite observation window.  This is consistent with the
non-uniform convergence as $\Sigma_0$ is approached.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Generic Lorentzian split]
\label{rem:generic-split}
For a generic Lorentzian $G$, the leaf dynamics is logistic rather than
exactly exponential.  The linearized rate at $K=1$ takes the form
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:generic-rate}
        \rho(\hat c)
        =
        \rho_0+\kappa\hat c^2 .
\end{equation}
In the diagonal Lorentzian gauge below, $\kappa>0$ follows symbolically.
For general non-diagonal $G$, the same positivity is verified in the
reproduction script over random Lorentzian samples; a fully
coordinate-free closed form for this positivity is left as part of the
gauge-independent formulation in Open Problem~6.  Here $\hat c$ denotes
the unit-normalized conserved charge associated with the left null
covector of $A_c$.  The constant term satisfies
\[
        \rho_0=-\beta,
\]
where $\beta$ is the quadratic logistic coefficient.  Thus the failure of
the exact exponential law is measured by the same coefficient that controls
the nonzero intercept of the rate law.

In a diagonal Lorentzian gauge
\[
        G=\operatorname{diag}(a,-b),
        \qquad
        a>0,
        \qquad
        b>0,
\]
one obtains the closed forms
\[
        \beta
        =
        \frac{2(b-a)}{\sqrt{ab}},
        \qquad
        \rho_0
        =
        \frac{2(a-b)}{\sqrt{ab}},
        \qquad
        \kappa
        =
        \frac{2(a+b)^2}{\sqrt{ab}}
        >
        0 .
\]
The gauge-fixed diagnostic
\[
        \chi_{\mathrm{unit}}
        =
        u^TG^{-1}u
        =
        \frac{b-a}{ab}
\]
has the same zero set as $\beta$, and
\[
        \frac{\rho_0}{\chi_{\mathrm{unit}}}
        =
        -2\sqrt{ab}
        =
        -2\sqrt{-\det G}.
\]
Therefore
\[
        \beta=0
        \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
        \rho_0=0
        \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
        \chi_{\mathrm{unit}}=0
        \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
        a=b .
\]
The balanced-canonical condition is exactly the condition under which the
generic rate law collapses to a pure $\hat c^2$ law.

Off the balanced gauge, two cases arise.  If
\[
        \rho_0<0,
\]
then there is a finite repelling band
\[
        |\hat c|<\hat c_{\mathrm{crit}},
        \qquad
        \hat c_{\mathrm{crit}}
        =
        \sqrt{-\rho_0/\kappa},
\]
inside which $\{K=1\}$ is not attracting.  If
\[
        \rho_0>0,
\]
there is no small-$\hat c$ repelling band, but the $\hat c=0$ leaf is still
not a zero-rate frozen leaf.  In both cases the exact exponential law, the
pure $c^2$ or $\hat c^2$ rate, the $\Theta(c^{-2})$ recovery time, and the
coincidence
\[
        \Sigma_0=\{c=0\}
\]
of Corollary~\ref{prop:recovery} are broken.

The diagnostic $\chi_{\mathrm{unit}}$ is defined relative to the Euclidean
gauge already fixed by the isotropic dissipation
\[
        D=d_cI
\]
of Law~III, and is not a coordinate scalar.  A gauge-independent
formulation of the same split is left open (cf.\ Open Problem~6).

In summary,
\[
        \text{critical null-flow is universal,}
        \qquad
        \text{exact }c^{-2}\text{ recovery is balanced-canonical.}
\]
Equivalently, Proposition~\ref{prop:nullflow-universal} gives the
universal theorem, while Corollary~\ref{prop:recovery} gives the
balanced-canonical specialization.
\end{remark}

\section{Structural path integral over cost geometries}\label{sec:scpi}

The preceding sections define the local cost-geometric and dynamical
content of the $K=1$ framework. We now record a statistical extension of
the same structure. This extension is not used in the derivation of
Laws~I--III and should not be read as a construction of the full
gravitational path integral. Its purpose is more limited: to formulate a
finite-dimensional structural path integral over admissible cost
geometries and to identify the measure-finiteness mechanism that appears
at the degenerate boundary $\det G\to0$. Throughout, the scope discipline
of \S\ref{sec:lawI} applies: the construction is a measure-finite
minisuperspace validator, not a derivation of the Einstein equations, and
the thermodynamic and algebraic routes retain their original conditional
status unaltered.

\subsection{Structural ensemble}

The ordinary path integral integrates over trajectories $x(t)$, with
weight $\exp(iS/\hbar)$~\cite{Feynman1948}; a formal gravitational path integral
integrates over spacetime metrics $g_{\mu\nu}$. In the present framework,
the natural ensemble variables are instead the cost-structure data
$(G,\Phi,H)$, where $G$ is the signed leading cost form associated with
the information-time layer, $\Phi$ is the structural variation parameter,
and $H>0$ is the scalar cost per unit variation. The structural path
integral is therefore written formally as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:scpi-Z}
Z_{\mathrm{SCPI}}
=\int_{\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{SC}}}
\mathcal{D}\mu_{\mathrm{SC}}[G,\Phi,H]\;
\exp\!\bigl(-S_{\mathrm{SC}}[G,\Phi,H]\bigr),
\end{equation}
over the structural support
\begin{equation}\label{eq:scpi-ensemble}
\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{SC}}
:=\bigl\{(G,\Phi,H):
\operatorname{Sig}(G)=(1,1),\;
\sigma_1(G,H)\ge\sigma_{1,c},\;
H>0\bigr\}.
\end{equation}
The first condition is supplied by Law~I at the level of the signed
leading form. The second is a structural support condition: subcritical
cost geometries are excluded from the ensemble outright, rather than
suppressed only perturbatively, with $\sigma_{1,c}=\hbar/(4\pi)$ the
self-consistency scale of Proposition~\ref{prop:domain}. In the present
minisuperspace validator, this soft self-consistency scale is imposed as
a support restriction in order to test the measure sector; this should
not be confused with an independently derived hard spacetime discreteness
scale (cf.\ the soft-bound caveat of \S\ref{sec:singularity}). The
information-time relation $dt_{\mathrm{info}}=d\Phi/H$ continues to hold,
so the ensemble is not an integral over trajectories parametrised by an
external time, but over the cost-structural data themselves.

\subsection{Finite-dimensional DeWitt-type measure}

To test the measure sector in a finite-dimensional model, restrict to
symmetric $2\times2$ cost forms and introduce a DeWitt-type
supermetric~\cite{DeWitt1967} on variations $\delta G$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:scpi-gram}
\mathcal{G}_{\alpha\beta}(G)
=\operatorname{tr}\!\bigl(G^{-1}S_\alpha\,G^{-1}S_\beta\bigr),
\qquad
\rho(G)=\sqrt{|\det\mathcal{G}(G)|},
\end{equation}
where $\{S_\alpha\}$ is a basis of symmetric $2\times2$ matrices. In this
minisuperspace model the density reduces to
\begin{equation}\label{eq:scpi-density}
\rho(G)\propto|\det G|^{-3/2}.
\end{equation}
The exponent $-3/2$ is fixed by the supermetric~\eqref{eq:scpi-gram} and
is verified numerically below. Because $-3/2<-1$, the bare structural
measure is singular at the degenerate boundary $\det G\to0$. Writing
$u:=|\det G|$, the reduced bare measure contains the non-integrable factor
$u^{-3/2}$, so with an infrared cutoff $u_{\max}$ and a near-degenerate
cutoff $\epsilon$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:scpi-bare}
Z_{\mathrm{bare}}(\epsilon)
=\int_{\epsilon}^{u_{\max}}u^{-3/2}\,du
=2\bigl(\epsilon^{-1/2}-u_{\max}^{-1/2}\bigr)
\xrightarrow{\;\epsilon\to0\;}\infty .
\end{equation}
The divergence is a genuine near-degenerate (small-$|\det G|$) effect, not
a large-geometry one: at large $u$ the integrand decays as $u^{-3/2}$, so
$\int^{\infty}u^{-3/2}\,du$ converges.

\subsection{Barrier-regulated finiteness}

The SCPI extension introduces a structural barrier against degenerate cost
geometries,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:scpi-barrier}
S_{\mathrm{barrier}}(G)=\eta\,|\det G|^{-p},
\qquad \eta>0,\;p>0,
\end{equation}
which suppresses near-degenerate geometries dynamically rather than by an
ad hoc hard cutoff. The reduced regulated measure becomes
\begin{equation}\label{eq:scpi-reg}
Z_{\mathrm{reg}}(\epsilon)
=\int_{\epsilon}^{u_{\max}}
u^{-3/2}\,\exp\!\bigl(-\eta\,u^{-p}\bigr)\,du .
\end{equation}
Since $\exp(-\eta u^{-p})$ suppresses the integrand faster than any inverse
power as $u\to0$, the integrand vanishes at the degenerate boundary, and
the regulated reduced measure is finite and independent of the
near-degenerate cutoff:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:scpi-finite}
\lim_{\epsilon\to0}Z_{\mathrm{reg}}(\epsilon)<\infty
\qquad\text{for all }\eta>0,\;p>0 .
\end{equation}
The same barrier also relocates the semiclassical saddle. Combining the
geometric weight $\alpha\,u$ with the barrier gives the reduced action
$S(u)=\alpha\,u+\eta\,u^{-p}$, whose stationarity condition
$dS/du=\alpha-\eta p\,u^{-p-1}=0$ has the unique interior solution
\begin{equation}\label{eq:scpi-saddle}
u_{*}=\left(\frac{\eta p}{\alpha}\right)^{1/(p+1)} .
\end{equation}
Thus the barrier does two things simultaneously: it renders the degenerate
boundary integrable, and it repels the semiclassical saddle away from the
degenerate locus $u=0$ to a non-degenerate interior
point~\eqref{eq:scpi-saddle}.

The central result of this section is not a single numerical value but the
existence of a \emph{robust measure-finite phase}: by~\eqref{eq:scpi-finite},
$Z_{\mathrm{reg}}$ is finite for every $\eta>0$, $p>0$, and a parameter
scan exhibits a cutoff-independent plateau across that phase (\S\ref{sec:scpi-num}).
For the default representative parameters
\begin{equation}\label{eq:scpi-default}
\eta=10^{-3},\qquad p=1,\qquad u_{\max}=1,
\end{equation}
the regulated integral approaches
\begin{equation}\label{eq:scpi-value}
Z_{\mathrm{reg}}\approx 54.05 .
\end{equation}
This value is independent of the near-degenerate cutoff $\epsilon$, but it
is \emph{not} a parameter-free universal constant: it depends on $\eta$,
$p$, $u_{\max}$, and the overall measure normalisation.

\subsection{Finite-dimensional numerical diagnostics}\label{sec:scpi-num}

The finite-dimensional diagnostics support the mechanism above. All are
either deterministic one-dimensional quadratures or direct evaluations;
Monte Carlo is unreliable on the near-degenerate tail, where exploding
variance is itself the signature of the bare divergence, so the
authoritative checks are deterministic.

\medskip\noindent\textit{(1) DeWitt density scaling.} A log--log fit of the
density~\eqref{eq:scpi-gram} against $|\det G|$ gives
\[
\log\rho(G)\;\text{vs}\;\log|\det G|:\quad\mathrm{slope}=-1.5000,
\]
matching the predicted exponent $-3/2$ of~\eqref{eq:scpi-density}.

\medskip\noindent\textit{(2) Effective-sample-size collapse (a caution, not
a result).} Reweighting by the bare density has a severely collapsed
effective sample size,
\[
\mathrm{ESS}=2.3\ \text{of}\ 4000,
\qquad \mathrm{ESS}/N=0.001,
\]
showing that the bare DeWitt reweighting is dominated by a handful of
near-degenerate configurations. This collapse is presented as diagnostic
evidence that the bare measure is pathological and \emph{requires} the
structural barrier, not as a positive result.

\medskip\noindent\textit{(3) Unregulated loop blow-up (scalar, not
graviton).} As an auxiliary illustration of why a regulator is needed at
all, a real $4$-dimensional Euclidean scalar one-loop integral was
evaluated against its closed form: a converged reference value
$I(\Lambda_{\mathrm{UV}})=0.995336$ is recovered, whereas at fixed cutoff
$\Lambda_{\mathrm{UV}}=1000$ the unregulated integral gives
$I(1000)=6332.5638$, a ratio $I(1000)/I(\Lambda_{\mathrm{UV}})=6362.24$
exhibiting the expected quadratic-type blow-up. We stress that this is a
\emph{scalar} loop, not a graviton loop, and is included only to
illustrate the unregulated-divergence pathology that motivates the
barrier philosophy; it makes no gravitational claim.

\medskip\noindent\textit{(4) Semiclassical saddle (default point).} For the
reduced action $S(u)=\alpha u+\eta u^{-p}$ with the default
parameters~\eqref{eq:scpi-default} and $\alpha=1$, deterministic
minimisation at the default point gives
\[
u_{\mathrm{numeric}}=u_{*}=0.03162,
\]
in agreement with~\eqref{eq:scpi-saddle} to relative error
$3.45\times10^{-5}$, with residual gradient
$dS/du(u_{*})\approx2.22\times10^{-16}$ (machine precision). This is the
default-point deterministic saddle check; the maximum relative error over
the full $\alpha$-sweep is reported separately in item~(9) below.

\medskip\noindent\textit{(5) Measure finiteness.} The bare integral follows
the predicted power law,
\[
Z_{\mathrm{bare}}\sim\epsilon^{-1/2},\qquad\mathrm{slope}=-0.5017,
\]
in agreement with~\eqref{eq:scpi-bare}, while the regulated integral with
the default parameters~\eqref{eq:scpi-default} converges to
\[
Z_{\mathrm{reg}}=54.050578,\qquad
\max\Delta Z=1.51\times10^{-7},
\]
the cutoff-stability indicator $\max\Delta Z$ being the maximum successive
change over the near-degenerate cutoff sweep of item~(7). (An earlier
diagnostic reported a relative plateau spread of $2.02\times10^{-6}$ over
the deepest cutoff decade; the present cutoff-stability figure is the
$\epsilon$-sweep $\max\Delta Z$.)

\medskip\noindent\textit{(6) Phase scan.} An $8\times8$ scan over
$(\eta,p)$ confirms the measure-finite phase: $57$ cases pass directly,
$7$ are slow-converging but integrable (the plateau appears only at deeper
cutoffs, where the barrier is weak), and crucially
\[
\text{PASS}=57,\qquad\text{SLOW}=7,\qquad\text{FAIL}=0
\qquad(\epsilon_{\min}=10^{-10},\;u_{\max}=1).
\]
The absence of failures, rather than any single value, is the substantive
finding.

\medskip\noindent\textit{(7) Near-degenerate cutoff sweep.} Refining
$\epsilon_{\min}\in\{10^{-6},10^{-8},10^{-10},10^{-12}\}$ at the default
parameters all give $Z_{\mathrm{reg}}\approx54.050578$, with maximum
successive change $\Delta Z=1.51\times10^{-7}$, confirming
$\epsilon$-independence.

\medskip\noindent\textit{(8) Infrared cutoff sweep.} Varying
$u_{\max}\in\{0.1,1,10,100\}$ gives
$Z_{\mathrm{reg}}=49.7464,\,54.0506,\,55.4175,\,55.8499$, respectively.
Since the large-$u$ tail $\int_{u_{\max}}^{\infty}u^{-3/2}\,du
=2u_{\max}^{-1/2}\to0$, the reduced measure is finite at the
large-geometry end as well as at the degenerate end.

\medskip\noindent\textit{(9) Saddle scaling.} For $p=1$ a sweep of
$\alpha\in[10^{-3},10^{3}]$ gives
\[
\log u_{*}\;\text{vs}\;\log\alpha:\quad\mathrm{slope}=-0.5000,
\]
matching the theoretical exponent $-1/(p+1)=-1/2$ of~\eqref{eq:scpi-saddle},
with maximum relative saddle error
\[
\max_{\alpha\text{-sweep}}\frac{|u_{\mathrm{numeric}}-u_{*}|}{u_{*}}
=6.91\times10^{-5}
\]
across the sweep (to be distinguished from the default-point error
$3.45\times10^{-5}$ of item~(4)). The barrier-regulated SCPI sector
therefore does not merely suppress a divergence; it also generates a
non-degenerate internal scale.

\subsection{Scope}

The construction above is a finite-dimensional minisuperspace validator
for the structural path-integral idea. It does \emph{not} construct the
full infinite-dimensional measure $\mathcal{D}[g]$, does \emph{not}
implement the quotient by $\mathrm{Diff}(M)$, does \emph{not} include the
graviton or Faddeev--Popov ghost sector~\cite{FaddeevPopov1967}, and does
\emph{not} prove perturbative finiteness of quantum gravity --- which is
known to fail for pure gravity at two loops~\cite{GoroffSagnotti1986}, so
no such claim is made or implied here. Its contribution is narrower but
nontrivial: the reduced DeWitt-type measure in the finite-dimensional
cost-geometry minisuperspace becomes finite after the structural barrier
is included, admitting a robust barrier-regulated finite phase at the
degenerate boundary together with an interior structural
scale~\eqref{eq:scpi-saddle}. In this respect the construction is
analogous in spirit to finite reduced or discrete path-integral models,
without inheriting their stronger claims. The barrier exponent $p$ and
weight $\eta$ are at present inputs; whether they can be fixed by the cost
axioms (Axioms~R, E, T), in the spirit of the $\eta$-weight question of
Appendix~\ref{app:eta}, is left open (cf.\ Open Problem~7). The route is

\section{Spacetime thermodynamics (thermodynamic perspective)}\label{sec:thermo}

This section develops the thermodynamic perspective as a conditional bridge to the Einstein equations. Every external identification used is marked explicitly; the full input list is summarised at the end of the section.

\medskip\noindent\textit{Sign convention and object split.} In this perspective we use the spacetime-sign convention
\[
g=\mathrm{diag}(-(\kappa\ell)^{2},1)
\]
for direct comparison with Rindler geometry. Statements depending only on $\det g$, $d_{c}$, or the hyperbolic spectrum are invariant under the overall sign change. Statements invoking Axiom~T as positive temporal cost are to be read in the cost-sign convention
\[
G_{\mathrm{cost}}=-g=\mathrm{diag}((\kappa\ell)^{2},-1),
\]
used explicitly in \S~\ref{sec:singularity}. Both conventions have determinant $-(\kappa\ell)^{2}$ and give the same $d_{c}$.

In this section, $g_{\mu\nu}$ denotes the physical Rindler metric used as the external spacetime background. By contrast, the quadratic form $G$ entering
\[
K=x^{\top}Gx,\qquad J_G=\alpha G^{-1}J,
\]
and the OU reduction is the cost-form used by the framework. In the Rindler realisation, these are related by the sign convention discussed in \S\ref{sec:lawI}, but they should not be conflated notationally: $g$ denotes the physical spacetime metric, whereas $G$ denotes the dynamical cost-form. In all OU and thermodynamic formulas below, $G$ denotes the dynamical cost-form, whereas $g$ denotes the external spacetime metric.

As a concrete realisation, we apply the framework to the Rindler metric
\[
g=\mathrm{diag}(-(\kappa\ell)^{2},1)
\]
\textup{[external: $g$ is the standard Rindler metric from GR, not derived from Law~I]},
where $\kappa$ is the surface gravity and $\ell$ the proper distance
from the horizon. The Rindler expressions are understood for a non-extremal local horizon, $\kappa>0$, and for $\ell>0$. Equation~\eqref{eq:dc} gives $d_{c}=\alpha/(\kappa\ell)$ (i.e., $\alpha/\sqrt{|g_{00}|}$ with $g_{00}=-(\kappa\ell)^{2}$ the temporal component of the Rindler metric). The expression for $d_{c}$ uses the diagonal/isotropic threshold described in \S\ref{sec:stability}; for general $H_{*}$, the threshold may shift. With $T_{H}=\hbar\kappa/(2\pi)$~\cite{Hawking},
\begin{equation}
d_{c}\cdot T_{H} = \frac{\alpha}{\kappa\ell}\cdot\frac{\hbar\kappa}{2\pi} = \frac{\alpha\hbar}{2\pi\ell},\label{eq:nearhorizon}
\end{equation}
exhibiting exact $\kappa$-cancellation. Hence the internal algebraic identity $d_{c}\cdot T_{H}=\alpha\hbar/(2\pi\ell)$ matches the Tolman temperature~\cite{Tolman} $T_{\mathrm{tol}}=\alpha\hbar/(2\pi\ell)$, depending only on the proper distance $\ell$ from the horizon, not on the surface gravity $\kappa$. This is an internal algebraic identity; it does not independently derive the Tolman temperature, but establishes a bridge between the framework's scale $d_{c}$ and the known thermal scale of the horizon.

\subsection*{Clausius relation (from OU dynamics) within Class~A}

In this subsection we work within the Sekimoto--Seifert stochastic thermodynamics framework~\cite{Sekimoto}; within that external framework, the key additional external input is the identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$, which is assumed here and not derived from the present framework.

\medskip\noindent\textit{Scope of the OU reduction.} The OU reduction below presupposes a strictly positive restoring rate toward $\{K=1\}$. It therefore applies wherever that rate is strictly positive, and degenerates where the restoring rate vanishes or where the leaf is non-attracting. In the balanced-canonical case this excludes the freeze leaf $\Sigma_0$ of Corollary~\ref{prop:recovery}; for generic Lorentzian $G$, the corresponding non-attracting set is described by the rate split of Remark~\ref{rem:generic-split}. All thermodynamic statements of this section are accordingly understood on the attracting, non-degenerate part of the dynamics.

Within Class~A (fixed inertial observer), and at a fixed $G$-eigenvector base point on $\{K=1\}$, the normal $K$-dynamics near $K=1$ reduces to a one-dimensional Ornstein--Uhlenbeck process. Its stationary variance is
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Var}(K)\approx\frac{\sigma^{2}}{2d_{c}},\label{eq:var}
\end{equation}
where the base-point factor cancels between the effective restoring rate and the induced $K$-space noise amplitude.
With this stationary variance, the FDT form $\sigma_{K}^{2}=2\kappa_{K}T_{\mathrm{eff}}$ defines an effective temperature
\[
T_{\mathrm{eff}}=\frac{\sigma^{2}}{2d_{c}},
\]
consistent with Law~II and Theorem~\ref{thm:restoring}. This is, at this stage, a formal effective-temperature definition: its identification with a physical horizon temperature requires the external input $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ introduced above. The Fokker--Planck equation then yields a Boltzmann stationary distribution $\rho\propto\exp(-V/T_{\mathrm{eff}})$; assuming the external identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$, the Clausius relation $\delta Q=T_{\mathrm{tol}}\,dS$ follows from local detailed balance for the OU process, with
\[
\delta Q := dV + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma_{K}^{2}\,dt,\qquad \sigma_{K}=2\sigma\|Gx_{*}\|.
\]
(The heat definition follows the Sekimoto--Seifert stochastic thermodynamics convention~\cite{Sekimoto}.) A derivation of the identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ from a more fundamental principle (e.g.\ a KMS condition~\cite{Haag,Connes1994}) remains an open problem.

\medskip\noindent
The following statement is conditional on the external identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$; it is not an autonomous derivation of the Clausius relation, but the equivalence theorem establishing what $\sigma^{2}$ must be when that identification is given.

\begin{proposition}[Local Clausius Equivalence]\label{prop:clausius}
Assume (i) the OU approximation near $K=1$, (ii) the Sekimoto--Seifert heat convention, (iii) local detailed balance for the OU process, and (iv) the \emph{external} identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$, together with the Tolman identity $T_{\mathrm{tol}}=d_{c}T_{H}$ (precise forms in the Remark below). Then the Clausius relation $\delta Q=T_{\mathrm{tol}}\,dS$ is equivalent to
\begin{equation}
\sigma^{2} = 2\,d_{c}\cdot T_{\mathrm{tol}} = 2\,d_{c}^{2}\cdot T_{H}.\label{eq:noise}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
$(\Rightarrow)$: Setting $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}=d_{c}T_{H}$ gives $\sigma^{2}/(2d_{c})=d_{c}\cdot T_{H}$, hence $\sigma^{2}=2d_{c}^{2}\cdot T_{H}$.
$(\Leftarrow)$: $\sigma^{2}=2d_{c}\cdot T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ gives $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=\sigma^{2}/(2d_{c})=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$, hence Clausius holds.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Assumptions of Proposition~\ref{prop:clausius}]
The assumptions entering Proposition~\ref{prop:clausius} are:
(i)~the OU approximation near $K=1$ ($|K-1|\ll 1$, equivalently $T_{\mathrm{eff}}\ll 1$);
(ii)~the Sekimoto--Seifert heat convention $\delta Q := dV + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma_{K}^{2}\,dt$;
(iii)~local detailed balance for the OU process;
(iv)~the external identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$;
together with the algebraic Tolman identity $T_{\mathrm{tol}}=d_{c}T_{H}$ ($\kappa$-factors cancel in eq.~\eqref{eq:nearhorizon}).
\end{remark}

\subsection*{Position-dependent temperature and It\^{o} corrections}

Applying It\^{o}'s formula to $K=x^{\top}Gx$ gives an additional drift $\sigma^{2}\,\mathrm{tr}(G)\,dt$. This shifts the mean of $K$ but not its variance: at any $G$-eigenvector base point, the FDT ratio $\sigma_{K}^{2}/(2\kappa_{K})$ reduces to $\sigma^{2}/(2d_{c})$ regardless of $x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}$. Hence~\eqref{eq:var} and $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=\sigma^{2}/(2d_{c})$ hold for any Lorentzian $G$.

The $K$-space noise amplitude $\sigma_{K}(x)=2\sigma\|Gx\|$ varies along $\{K=1\}$, yielding a position-dependent temperature (eq.~\eqref{eq:Tloc}):
\begin{equation}
T_{\mathrm{loc}}(x) := \frac{\sigma_{K}(x)^{2}}{2\kappa_{K}(x_{*})} = \frac{\sigma^{2}\,x^{\top}G^{2}x}{2d_{c}\,x_{*}^{\top}G^{2}x_{*}}.\label{eq:Tloc}
\end{equation}
This position dependence should not be read as a Class~B heat fluctuation: variation along the boost orbit changes the observer/position assignment, while Class~B remains thermodynamically inert and carries no heat.

\begin{remark}[Stability constraint]
For macroscopic black holes, $T_{\mathrm{tol}}\ll d_{c}$; setting $D=T_{\mathrm{tol}}I$ renders $K=1$ unstable. Stability requires $D\ge d_{c}I$; critical damping $D=d_{c}I$ is the unique marginally stable choice.
\end{remark}

Since every local Rindler horizon admits the standard Rindler approximation~\cite{Jacobson} with $g=\mathrm{diag}(-(\kappa\ell)^{2},1)$ (where $\kappa$ and $\ell$ vary by location), Proposition~\ref{prop:clausius} applies to each such horizon, yielding the Clausius relation locally. Together with Jacobson's argument~\cite{Jacobson}, this supplies the local Clausius input needed for the Einstein equations. Once the external identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ has been given, the remaining external input required to invoke Jacobson's argument is $S\propto A$.

\medskip
\noindent
\textbf{Summary of logical status.}
The thermodynamic perspective is a conditional bridge rather than an autonomous derivation of the Einstein equations. Its internal inputs are the Lorentzian structure supplied by Law~I, the isotropic stability scale $d_{c}$, and the OU dynamics of Class~A near $K=1$. To connect these ingredients to Jacobson's argument, two external inputs are required: (i) the identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$, and (ii) the entropy--area law $S\propto A$. With these added, the framework supplies a local Clausius relation on each local Rindler horizon, thereby providing the local Clausius input used in Jacobson's route to $R_{\mu\nu}-\tfrac12 R\,g_{\mu\nu}=8\pi T_{\mu\nu}$.

\section{An algebraic reformulation of vacuum Einstein in the spherical sector}\label{sec:field}

We now consider, as a candidate field-level ansatz (not derived from Laws~I--III, cf.\ Open Problem~1), the differential conditions $K_1=K_2=1$, where $K_i:=\sigma_2^2\Box\ln\sigma_i$ are constructed from the symplectic eigenvalues $\sigma_1:=r\sqrt{f}$ and $\sigma_2:=r$ of the spherically symmetric metric. These conditions are introduced as a candidate field-level ansatz, not derived from Laws~I--III. The main result of this section is Theorem~\ref{thm:field}: in the spherical sector with $f>0$, this ansatz is algebraically equivalent to $R_{\mu\nu}=0$. The result therefore provides a cost-structural reformulation of vacuum Einstein in the spherical sector, not a derivation of Einstein from the framework axioms.

All statements below are local on intervals where $f>0$, so that $\sigma_1=r\sqrt{f}$ and $\ln\sigma_1$ are real. Consider the static spherically symmetric metric
\begin{equation}\label{eq:metric}
  ds^2=-f(r)\,dt^2+f(r)^{-1}dr^2+r^2\,d\Omega^2,
\end{equation}
with symplectic eigenvalues $\sigma_1:=r\sqrt{f}$, $\sigma_2:=r$
(on each local Rindler wedge, $d_{c}\sigma_1=\alpha$, connecting $\sigma_1$
to the $2\times 2$ symplectic eigenvalue of~\S\ref{sec:symplectic}),
and d'Alembertian
$\Box\phi=r^{-2}\partial_r(r^2f\,\partial_r\phi)$
(the radial d'Alembertian, exact for $r$-dependent $\phi$;
primes denote $d/dr$).

\begin{definition}[Eigenvalue self-consistency functionals]\label{def:esc}
\begin{align}
  K_1 &:= \sigma_2^2\,\Box\ln\sigma_1,\label{eq:K1def}\\
  K_2 &:= \sigma_2^2\,\Box\ln\sigma_2.\label{eq:K2def}
\end{align}
\end{definition}

\noindent
The differential conditions $K_i=1$ for $i=1,2$ require that the
d'Alembertian of each log-eigenvalue equals $1/\sigma_2^2$. We adopt
these conditions as a candidate field-level ansatz; whether they admit
an independent justification from Laws~I--III remains open (cf.\ Open
Problem~1 below). The flavour of the ansatz is similar to the point-level
$K=1$ — each eigenvalue is required to satisfy a self-consistency
condition involving its own logarithm — but the field-level conditions
are not derived from the point-level ones.

Expanding (direct computation using $\Box\ln\sigma_1=\Box\ln r+\tfrac{1}{2}\Box\ln f$):
\begin{equation}\label{eq:K1exp}
  K_1 = f+2rf'+\tfrac{r^2}{2}f'',
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:K2exp}
  K_2 = rf'+f = \frac{d(rf)}{dr}.
\end{equation}
(For $K_2$: $\Box\ln r = (1/r^2)d/dr[r^2f/r]=(rf'+f)/r^2$,
so $r^2\Box\ln r = rf'+f$.)

\begin{lemma}[Scalar Curvature Formula]\label{lem:R}
$R=-2\,\Box\ln\sigma_1+2/r^2$ for~\eqref{eq:metric}.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
$R=-f''-4f'/r+2(1{-}f)/r^2$.
$\Box\ln\sigma_1=f/r^2+2f'/r+f''/2$ (from $K_1=r^2\Box\ln\sigma_1$ and eq.~\eqref{eq:K1exp}).
Then $-2\Box\ln\sigma_1+2/r^2=R$.\qed
\end{proof}

\medskip\noindent
The following theorem establishes an algebraic equivalence between the candidate ansatz and the vacuum Einstein equations in the spherical sector. It is a reformulation of vacuum Einstein in cost-structural language, conditional on adopting $K_i=1$ as a field-level ansatz.

\begin{theorem}[Algebraic Equivalence]\label{thm:field}
For~\eqref{eq:metric} with $f\in C^{2}(I)$ and $f>0$ on an interval $I\subset(0,\infty)$ (so that $\sigma_{1}=r\sqrt{f}$ and $\ln\sigma_{1}$ are real), and $K_1,K_2$ as in Definition~\ref{def:esc}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:equiv}
  K_1=K_2=1
  \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad R_{\mu\nu}=0,
\end{equation}
where $R_{\mu\nu}=0$ are the vacuum Einstein field equations~\cite{Einstein1915,Hilbert1915}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$(\Rightarrow)$\;Suppose $K_1=K_2=1$ on $I$.
For this metric, the standard formulas give $R_{\theta\theta}=1-f-rf'$ and $R_{tt}=\frac{f}{2}(f''+2f'/r)$~\cite{Wald1984}. Using eq.~\eqref{eq:K2exp}, $R_{\theta\theta}=1-K_2$; with $K_2=1$ we obtain $R_{\theta\theta}=0$.
From eqs.~\eqref{eq:K1exp}--\eqref{eq:K2exp}, $K_1-K_2=rf'+\frac{r^2}{2}f''=0$, hence $f''=-2f'/r$. Substituting into $R_{tt}$ gives $R_{tt}=0$. For this metric $R_{tt}=-f^{2}R_{rr}$~\cite{Wald1984}, so $R_{tt}=0$ and $f>0$ give $R_{rr}=0$. The remaining diagonal component $R_{\phi\phi}=\sin^2\theta\cdot R_{\theta\theta}=0$ by spherical symmetry, and all off-diagonal Ricci components vanish identically by staticity and spherical symmetry. Hence $R_{\mu\nu}=0$.

$(\Leftarrow)$\;Suppose $R_{\mu\nu}=0$ on $I$.
The trace gives $R=g^{\mu\nu}R_{\mu\nu}=0$; by Lemma~\ref{lem:R}, $R=2(1-K_1)/r^2$, hence $K_1=1$.
$R_{\theta\theta}=0$ gives $K_2=1-R_{\theta\theta}=1$.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
The condition $K_1{=}1$ is the second-order linear ODE
$f+2rf'+\frac{r^2}{2}f''=1$, equivalent to $R{=}0$, with general solution
$f=1-C_1/r-C_2/r^2$; $K_2{=}1$ forces $C_2{=}0$,
recovering the Schwarzschild solution uniquely, in agreement with Birkhoff's theorem.
In the weak field ($f=1+\varepsilon\varphi$),
$K_2{=}1$ gives $\varphi=C/r$ (Newton) in one step;
$K_1{=}1$ is automatically satisfied at linear order,
so its independent content is purely nonlinear (strong-field).
\end{corollary}

\begin{lemma}[Asymmetric rigidity]\label{lem:rigidity}
For metric~\eqref{eq:metric} with $f\in C^{2}(I)$ on an interval $I\subset(0,\infty)$:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[\textup{(a)}] $K_{2}\equiv 1$ on $I$ if and only if $f=1+C/r$ for some constant $C$, in which case $K_{1}\equiv 1$ on $I$ as well.
\item[\textup{(b)}] $K_{1}\equiv 1$ on $I$ if and only if $f=1+A/r+B/r^{2}$ for some constants $A,B$. Substituting into the definition of $K_{2}$ gives
\[
K_{2}=1-B/r^{2},
\]
so the converse of (a) fails when $B\neq 0$: $K_{1}\equiv 1$ does not imply $K_{2}\equiv 1$.
\end{enumerate}
Thus $K_{2}\equiv 1$ is the strictly stronger condition; the two-parameter family $f=1+A/r+B/r^{2}$ satisfies $K_{1}\equiv 1$ but generically violates $K_{2}\equiv 1$. Reissner--Nordstr\"{o}m is one such non-vacuum example.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
$(a)$\;$K_{2}=f+rf'=d(rf)/dr$, so $K_{2}\equiv 1$ is the first-order ODE $d(rf)/dr=1$ with general solution $rf=r+C$, i.e.\ $f=1+C/r$. Direct substitution then gives $K_{1}=(1+C/r)+2r(-C/r^{2})+(r^{2}/2)(2C/r^{3})=1+C/r-2C/r+C/r=1$.\;
$(b)$\;$K_{1}\equiv 1$ is the second-order linear ODE $f+2rf'+(r^{2}/2)f''=1$, which is the Euler-type equation having Schwarzschild--Reissner-Nordstr\"{o}m form general solution $f=1+A/r+B/r^{2}$. Substituting into $K_{2}=f+rf'$ gives $K_{2}=(1+A/r+B/r^{2})+r(-A/r^{2}-2B/r^{3})=1-B/r^{2}$. Hence $K_{2}\equiv 1$ requires $B=0$.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Asymmetric roles of $K_1$ and $K_2$]
Lemma~\ref{lem:rigidity} formalises the asymmetry: $K_{2}\equiv 1$ is rigid (forces $K_{1}\equiv 1$ via $f=1+C/r$), whereas $K_{1}\equiv 1$ is flexible (admits a one-parameter family of $K_{2}$-violating solutions). This asymmetry underlies the testability discussion of Open Problem~3.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Observational implications]
Within the present spherical-vacuum analysis, any observational distinction between the eigenvalue principle and standard GR must therefore arise from strong-gravity data.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}[$\Lambda$-extension of Theorem~\ref{thm:field}]\label{cor:SdS}
For metric~\eqref{eq:metric} with $f\in C^{2}(I)$ and $f>0$ on $I\subset(0,\infty)$, and $K_{1},K_{2}$ as in Definition~\ref{def:esc}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Lambda-equiv}
K_{1}=1-2\Lambda r^{2}\;\;\text{and}\;\;K_{2}=1-\Lambda r^{2}
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad R_{\mu\nu}=\Lambda g_{\mu\nu}.
\end{equation}
The general solution is the Schwarzschild--de~Sitter family $f=1-2M/r-\Lambda r^{2}/3$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
$(\Rightarrow)$\;
$K_{2}=1-\Lambda r^{2}$ rewrites as $f+rf'=1-\Lambda r^{2}$, i.e.\ $d(rf)/dr=1-\Lambda r^{2}$, with general solution $rf=r-\Lambda r^{3}/3+\text{const}$, so $f=1-2M/r-\Lambda r^{2}/3$ for some constant $M$. Direct substitution then gives $K_{1}=1-2\Lambda r^{2}$ automatically. For this $f$, $R_{\theta\theta}=1-K_{2}=\Lambda r^{2}=\Lambda g_{\theta\theta}$ and $R_{tt}=(f/r^{2})(K_{1}-K_{2})=-\Lambda f=\Lambda g_{tt}$. For this metric $R_{tt}=-f^{2}R_{rr}$~\cite{Wald1984}, so $R_{tt}=\Lambda g_{tt}$ is equivalent to $R_{rr}=\Lambda g_{rr}$; the angular components are fixed by $R_{\theta\theta}=\Lambda g_{\theta\theta}$ and spherical symmetry ($R_{\phi\phi}=\sin^{2}\!\theta\,R_{\theta\theta}=\Lambda g_{\phi\phi}$), closing all four nontrivial Ricci components.\;
$(\Leftarrow)$\;
$R_{\theta\theta}=\Lambda g_{\theta\theta}=\Lambda r^{2}$ gives $K_{2}=1-\Lambda r^{2}$. $R_{tt}=\Lambda g_{tt}=-\Lambda f$ combined with $R_{tt}=(f/r^{2})(K_{1}-K_{2})$ gives $K_{1}-K_{2}=-\Lambda r^{2}$, hence $K_{1}=1-2\Lambda r^{2}$.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Matter and the role of $\Lambda$]
From the $tt$ component of the Einstein equations,
$\rho = (1{-}K_2)/(8\pi r^2) = (1-\sigma_2^2\,\Box\ln\sigma_2)/(8\pi\sigma_2^2)$:
matter may be read as the angular eigenvalue's failure to self-consistently
propagate, measured per unit area.
This relation \emph{uses} the Einstein equations;
it is a cost rewriting, not an independent derivation
(in particular, $8\pi$ is inherited from GR).
The value of $\Lambda$ is not predicted by the present framework.
\end{remark}

\subsection*{Logical status of \S\ref{sec:field}}

The reader may wonder whether Theorem~\ref{thm:field} constitutes a
derivation of vacuum Einstein from the $K{=}1$ framework. It does not.
The theorem establishes an algebraic equivalence between two systems
of differential conditions: $K_1=K_2=1$ on one side, and $R_{\mu\nu}=0$
on the other. The conditions $K_i=1$ are introduced at the field level
as an ansatz; the framework's axioms (Laws~I--III) constrain the
algebraic type of $G$ at the point level but do not, in their present
formulation, single out the specific differential conditions $K_i=1$
from among the family of possible field-level uplifts of the point-level
balance $K=1$.

What \S\ref{sec:field} does establish is that \emph{if} one adopts $K_i=1$
as a field-level ansatz, then the vacuum Einstein equations admit an
equivalent formulation in cost-structural language. This is a
reformulation, not a derivation. Whether this particular ansatz is
uniquely natural --- and whether it admits an independent justification
beyond the algebraic equivalence --- remains open (cf.\ Open Problem~1).

\begin{remark}[Dynamical motivation, not derivation]
The point-level null-flow structure of \S\ref{sec:nullflow} shows that, at
critical damping, the locus $K=1$ is dynamically singled out as an
attractor on the non-degenerate leaves. This lends additional
\emph{motivation} to the appearance of $K=1$-type balance conditions, but
it does not bridge the point-level balance $K=x^{\top}Gx=1$ to the
field-level differential conditions $K_i=1$ of this section: the latter
remain an independent ansatz (cf.\ Open Problem~1). No logical dependence
of the present algebraic reformulation on the null-flow result is claimed.
\end{remark}

\section{Domain of validity and self-consistency resolution scale}\label{sec:singularity}

The following results concern the \emph{domain} of the framework rather than the physical existence of singularities: where $\sigma_1=0$, the $K{=}1$ locus is empty and the framework is undefined, not incorrect.

\begin{proposition}[Positivity from Axiom~T]\label{prop:sigma1}
Among the three axioms, only Axiom~T is used here; Axioms~R and~E are needed only in \S~\ref{sec:lawI} to establish $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$. In the $2\times 2$ wedge representation used here, Axiom~T implies $\sigma_1>0$ wherever $K{=}1$ holds.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For the $(t,r)$ wedge (and by the same argument for any $2\times 2$ Lorentzian $G$), $G=\mathrm{diag}(\sigma_1^2,-1)$: Axiom~T gives $G_{00}=\sigma_1^2>0$; contrast: $g_{00}=-f<0$ for the spacetime metric; the sign convention here differs from \S\ref{sec:thermo} but gives the same $d_{c}$, since $\det G=-\sigma_1^2$ in both cases (only $|\det G|$ and the positive-cost temporal direction enter the argument). If $\sigma_1=0$ then $G_{00}=0$, violating Axiom~T; with $\sigma_1$ taken as the positive scale factor (e.g., $\sigma_1=r\sqrt{f}$ on the real branch), this yields $\sigma_1>0$.\qed
\end{proof}

\noindent
A direct consequence: when $\sigma_1=0$, $K=-x_2^2\le 0$ and $K{=}1$ has no solutions. The Schwarzschild interior, including the singular limit $r\to 0$, therefore lies outside the domain of the framework: $\sigma_1=r\sqrt{f}$ is defined on the real branch $f>0$, so the interior $r<2M$ (where $f<0$) and the limit $r\to 0$ fall outside this domain --- the framework is undefined there, not incorrect.

\medskip\noindent
The following is a soft consistency bound from the OU validity regime, not a hard quantum-gravity cutoff.

\begin{proposition}[Self-consistency resolution scale]\label{prop:domain}
In Planck units ($\hbar=\ell_P=1$), under the Tolman-temperature identification, the OU approximation, and Schwarzschild near-horizon normalisation, thermal self-consistency of the $K{=}1$ framework requires $\sigma_1>\sigma_{1,c}:=\hbar/(4\pi)=\ell_P/(4\pi)$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
\textup{[External identification: $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$,
as in \S~\ref{sec:thermo}; all other steps are internal.]}
Near the horizon, $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}=\hbar\kappa/(2\pi\sqrt{f})$
(Tolman redshift factor; cf.~\eqref{eq:nearhorizon} with $\alpha=1$). With $\sigma_1=r\sqrt{f}$ and $r\approx 2M$, $\kappa=1/(4M)$ near the Schwarzschild horizon:
\begin{equation*}
T_{\mathrm{eff}}\approx\frac{\hbar\cdot(1/(4M))}{2\pi\cdot\sigma_1/(2M)}=\frac{\hbar}{4\pi\sigma_1}.
\end{equation*}
Equivalently, since $\ell\approx 2\sigma_1$ near the Schwarzschild horizon, this agrees with $T_{\mathrm{tol}}=\hbar/(2\pi\ell)$ from~\eqref{eq:nearhorizon} (with $\alpha=1$).
$\mathrm{Var}(K)\approx T_{\mathrm{eff}}$ (from eq.~\eqref{eq:var} and $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$, in the OU approximation); for $K{=}1$ to be a
well-defined equilibrium, $\mathrm{Var}(K)<1$, hence
$\sigma_1>\hbar/(4\pi)$. This is a self-consistent bound: the OU approximation must be valid, i.e., $\mathrm{Var}(K)<1$.\qed
\end{proof}

\noindent
Below $\sigma_{1,c}$, thermal fluctuations of $K$ become of order one, and the classical description of the $K{=}1$ locus dissolves into noise; this bound does not, by itself, imply a fundamental spacetime discreteness.

\medskip\noindent
\textit{Scope of the bound.}
The derivation of $\sigma_{1,c}$ makes explicit use of the external identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$, which belongs to the thermodynamic perspective (\S\ref{sec:thermo}). Within the algebraic perspective, where no such thermal identification is invoked, whether an analogous self-consistency scale exists remains an open question. Turning the soft consistency bound into a hard uncertainty bound \emph{independent} of the thermodynamic identification is the subject of Open Problem~4 in \S\ref{sec:discussion}.

\medskip\noindent
\textit{Terminology.}

\subsection*{Two complementary perspectives: thermodynamic and algebraic}

Within the thermodynamic perspective, once the required external identifications are supplied, the Einstein equations are conditionally recovered via stochastic dynamics and the Jacobson argument (\S\ref{sec:thermo}). \subsection*{Scope of claims}

The thermodynamic perspective, given the external identifications $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ and $S\propto A$, conditionally recovers the Einstein equations $R_{\mu\nu}-\tfrac12 R\,g_{\mu\nu}=8\pi T_{\mu\nu}$ through the Jacobson argument. The algebraic perspective establishes, under the field-level ansatz $K_i=1$, an equivalence with $R_{\mu\nu}=0$ in the spherical sector, with no thermodynamic input but with the ansatz itself as a separate input. Thus, in the spherical vacuum sector, the vacuum equations admit an equivalent cost-structural formulation (Theorem~\ref{thm:field}), conditional on adopting $K_i=1$. The structural path integral of \S\ref{sec:scpi} is a separate, exploratory extension: it is a finite-dimensional minisuperspace validator of the barrier-regulated measure-finiteness mechanism and makes no claim about the full gravitational path integral.

The contribution of the present paper is therefore conceptual and structural: Lorentzian signature is supplied internally by the cost-realizability bridge of Law~I, rather than assumed as a kinematic primitive; Theorem~\ref{thm:equivalence} provides its isotropic stability reading. Neither the matter sector of the Einstein equations nor the thermal inputs needed for the Jacobson route are derived autonomously within the present framework; in particular, the entropy--area law remains external, and the matter sector is recovered via the thermodynamic perspective only when the two external identifications are supplied.

Accordingly, the matter density $\rho=(1-K_2)/(8\pi r^2)$ and the interpretation of matter are GR rewritings; $\Lambda$ is not predicted by the framework; $S\propto A$ remains an external input borrowed from black-hole thermodynamics. The algebraic perspective in its current form is conditional on the field-level ansatz; whether this ansatz can be derived from the framework's axioms, rather than introduced as a separate assumption, remains open.

\subsection*{Structural path integral as a fourth, exploratory layer}

The SCPI construction of \S\ref{sec:scpi} suggests a statistical extension of the $K=1$ framework in which one integrates over admissible cost geometries rather than over trajectories or unconstrained metrics. The finite-dimensional test carried out there identifies a robust measure-finiteness mechanism: the bare DeWitt-type density $\rho(G)\propto|\det G|^{-3/2}$ is non-integrable near $\det G=0$, while the structural barrier $S_{\mathrm{barrier}}=\eta|\det G|^{-p}$ makes the reduced measure finite and produces an interior saddle $u_{*}=(\eta p/\alpha)^{1/(p+1)}$. Extending this mechanism to a genuine field-theoretic, diffeomorphism-invariant measure remains open. In particular, a full theory would require a covariant definition of the structural measure, a treatment of the $\mathrm{Diff}(M)$ quotient, the analogue of the Faddeev--Popov or BRST sector, and a physical loop expansion for the cost-geometry fields. None of these is attempted here; the present contribution is the identification and validation of the finiteness mechanism in a minisuperspace setting.

\subsection*{Extension to $(1{+}3)$ dimensions}

The following remarks are heuristic and should be read as an outlook rather than a partial extension theorem. In $(1{+}3)$ dimensions, $\{K{=}1\}\cong H^3$ carries $S^2$ cross-sections with area $4\pi\sigma_1^2$ when $\alpha=1$ and $\sigma_1=\kappa\ell$, linking the symplectic invariant to the Bekenstein--Hawking area~\cite{Bekenstein}; the $1/4$ coefficient still requires the usual Hawking/first-law input. Two structural changes remain: Lorentzian signature alone is no longer sufficient to guarantee $d_c>0$, and $J_G$ requires a symplectic pairing choice. Whether the cost-realizability mechanism that forces $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$ in two dimensions admits a natural extension that constrains the full $(1{+}3)$-dimensional metric signature without assuming it, is an open conceptual question not addressed in this paper.

\subsection*{Limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
\item The algebraic perspective is currently confined to spherical symmetry, and depends on the field-level ansatz $K_i=1$ as a separate input not derived from Laws~I--III. \textit{(scope and ansatz status)}
\item The thermodynamic perspective still depends on the external inputs $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ and $S\propto A$. In particular, the identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$ is a phenomenological assumption and is not explained by the internal structure of Laws~I--III. \textit{(external input)}
\item No observational deviation from GR is obtained in the present analysis. \textit{(predictive gap)}
\item The $\sigma_1$ threshold derived in \S\ref{sec:singularity} is a soft consistency bound only, not a hard quantum-gravitational cutoff. \textit{(strength)}
\item The scalar $H$ (information-time layer) and the matrix weight $H_{*}$ (stability layer) are not unified in the present framework. \textit{(notation and layering)}
\item At critical damping the rank-one null-flow structure is universal (Proposition~\ref{prop:nullflow-universal}), while the exact exponential recovery and pure $c^2$ rate are balanced-canonical features (Corollary~\ref{prop:recovery}). For generic $G$, the leaf dynamics is logistic (Remark~\ref{rem:generic-split}). \textit{(OP6 split)}
\item The structural path integral of \S\ref{sec:scpi} is a finite-dimensional minisuperspace validator; the barrier weight $\eta$ and exponent $p$ are inputs, and the full infinite-dimensional, diffeomorphism-invariant measure is not constructed. \textit{(scope of the path-integral extension)}
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Open problems}

The first three problems concern structural completion and empirical distinctness; the next three concern internal dynamical and quantum consistency; the last concerns the path-integral extension.

\begin{enumerate}
\item Resolve the weight ambiguity of Appendix~\ref{app:eta}. The
relaxed $\eta$-weighted field action admits two natural prescriptions
for the single free weight $\eta$ that disagree: the Euler--Lagrange
marginal-mode reading gives $\eta=2$ (eq.~\eqref{eq:etaEL}), while the
field-level lift of Axiom~R, via a zero-cost mode, gives $\eta=1$
(eq.~\eqref{eq:etaR}). The framework as presently formulated supplies
no principle adjudicating between them. Determine whether such a
principle follows from the cost axioms (Axioms~R, E, T) or from an
independent structural requirement. This ambiguity is logically prior
to, and decides the status of, the conditional $\beta/r^3$ correction
of eq.~\eqref{eq:fform}: a resolution selecting $\eta=2$ would upgrade
that correction from conditional to predicted, whereas $\eta=1$ removes
it entirely. Independently of which value is selected, this also
subsumes the original question of justifying the field-level conditions
$K_i=1$, which correspond to the hard-constraint limit of \eqref{eq:Seta}.
\item Extend the algebraic perspective to non-vacuum matter. In particular, determine whether $\rho=(1-K_2)/(8\pi r^2)$ is an intrinsic consequence of the cost framework, rather than a GR rewriting, whether matter can thereby be understood as the failure of angular eigenvalue self-consistency per unit area, and whether this extends to the full stress-energy tensor~$T_{\mu\nu}$.
\item Identify testable strong-field predictions. Within the present spherical-vacuum analysis, since in the weak-field regime $K_2=1$ already reproduces the Newtonian potential while $K_1=1$ is automatic at linear order, any observational distinction between the eigenvalue principle and standard GR must arise from the nonlinear strong-field sector, i.e.\ from the term $B/r^2$ in the $K_1$-only family that distinguishes $K_1$-equivalence from pure Schwarzschild (Lemma~\ref{lem:rigidity}). Possible windows include high-precision black-hole shadow data, strong-field gravitational-wave signals, and near-horizon quantum effects.
\item Establish a first-principles relation between the motivating information time $dt_{\mathrm{info}}$ and the effective dynamical evolution parameter $t$ used in Laws~II--III and the thermodynamic perspective.
\item Prove $\sigma_{1,\min}\geq\hbar/(4\pi)$ as a hard bound from $K$--$\varphi$ canonical conjugacy \emph{without} invoking the thermodynamic identification $T_{\mathrm{eff}}=T_{\mathrm{tol}}$, where $\varphi$ is the boost angle along $\{K{=}1\}$.
\item Develop a gauge-independent and higher-dimensional formulation of the OP6 split. In the $2\times2$ Lorentzian setting, Proposition~\ref{prop:nullflow-universal} settles the universal part: at critical damping $A_c$ is rank-one, admits a conserved linear charge, and its image and kernel are the two $G$-null rays. Remark~\ref{rem:generic-split} also shows that the exact exponential law, pure $c^2$ rate, $\Theta(c^{-2})$ recovery, and the single frozen $c=0$ leaf are balanced-canonical special features. What remains open is to formulate this split without the gauge-fixed diagnostic $\chi_{\mathrm{unit}}$, to characterise the corresponding non-attracting sets invariantly, and to determine whether an analogue survives in $(1{+}3)$ dimensions once a symplectic pairing choice is supplied.
\item Determine whether the structural barrier $S_{\mathrm{barrier}}=\eta|\det G|^{-p}$ of \S\ref{sec:scpi} can be \emph{derived} from the cost axioms (Axioms~R, E, T) rather than imposed. A derivation fixing the exponent $p$ and weight $\eta$ would upgrade the interior structural scale $u_{*}=(\eta p/\alpha)^{1/(p+1)}$ from a parameter-dependent quantity to a prediction of the framework, and would parallel the $\eta$-weight question of Open Problem~1 at the level of the path-integral measure. Whether a covariant, diffeomorphism-invariant lift of the finite-dimensional measure of \S\ref{sec:scpi} exists is a further, logically subsequent question.
\end{enumerate}

\section{The realizability foundation: derivation of Lorentzian signature from cost asymmetry}\label{app:realizability}

This appendix establishes, in self-contained form, the result invoked throughout the main text: under three explicit axioms on a primitive cost function~$d$, the leading quadratic form~$Q$ extracted from~$d^{2}$ is forced to have Lorentzian signature $(1,1)$ in two dimensions, provided $Q$ is nondegenerate. The result was obtained in an earlier unpublished manuscript by the author; for the present paper to be logically self-contained, we reproduce the definitions, the key lemma, and the main theorem here in the form used by Law~I (\S\ref{sec:lawI}).

\subsection*{A.1\quad Setup}

Let $U\subset\mathbb{R}^{2}$ be open with chart $(t,r)$, and fix $x\in U$. For small $\delta x=(\delta t,\delta r)$ with $\delta t>0$, let $d(x;\delta x)\ge 0$ be a displacement cost at~$x$. The norm $\|\cdot\|$ denotes any norm on~$\mathbb{R}^{2}$; all conclusions are independent of the choice. Write $S^{1}=\{u\in\mathbb{R}^{2}:\|u\|=1\}$. The chart variables $t$ and~$r$ label a distinguished direction (carrying positive cost, Axiom~T) and its complement; no metric or causal structure on~$U$ is assumed at this stage.

\subsection*{A.2\quad The three axioms}

\begin{definition}[Axiom~R --- Zero-threshold realizability]\label{app:def:R}
There exists a sequence $\delta x_{n}=(\delta t_{n},\delta r_{n})\to 0$ with $\delta t_{n}>0$, $\delta r_{n}\neq 0$ such that
\[
\frac{d(x;\delta x_{n})}{\|\delta x_{n}\|}\longrightarrow 0,
\]
and there exists a subsequence $v_{n_{k}}\to v=(v_{t},v_{r})$ with $v_{t}>0$ and $v_{r}\neq 0$, where $v_{n_{k}}:=\delta x_{n_{k}}/\|\delta x_{n_{k}}\|$.
\end{definition}

The condition $\delta t_{n}>0$ restricts attention to forward-in-time displacements; the proof uses only one null direction, which the single-sided condition already provides.

\begin{definition}[Axiom~E --- Quadratic expansion]\label{app:def:E}
$d^{2}$ admits a quadratic leading-order expansion at~$x$: there exists a quadratic form $Q(\delta t,\delta r)=a\,\delta t^{2}+2\kappa\,\delta t\,\delta r+b\,\delta r^{2}$, representing the second-order behaviour of~$d^{2}$, such that
\[
d(x;\delta x)^{2} = \bigl(Q(\delta x)\bigr)_{+} + o\!\left(\|\delta x\|^{2}\right)
\quad\text{as } \delta x\to 0 \text{ with } \delta t>0,
\]
where $(u)_{+}:=\max\{u,0\}$ and the remainder $r(\rho u)/\rho^{2}\to 0$ as $\rho\downarrow 0$, uniformly for $u$ in compact subsets of $\{u\in S^{1}:u_{t}>0\}$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Axiom~T --- Temporal cost]\label{app:def:T}
There exists $c_{t}>0$ such that for all sufficiently small $\Delta t>0$,
\[
d\bigl(x;(\Delta t,0)\bigr)\ge c_{t}\,\Delta t.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Primitive status of $d$ and meaning of $(Q)_{+}$]
The logical structure is that $d$ is primitive and $Q$ is derived. Axioms~R and~T constrain~$d$ directly, while Axiom~E extracts from~$d^{2}$ a quadratic leading-order form~$Q$. This is what makes the Lorentzian signature theorem nontrivial: the geometry is not assumed in advance. The truncation $(Q)_{+}$ records the fact that only positive quadratic contributions of~$Q$ appear in the leading behaviour of~$d^{2}$. For a sequence $\delta x_{n}\to 0$ whose normalised directions converge to a vector~$v$ with $Q(v)\le 0$, we have $d(x;\delta x_{n})/\|\delta x_{n}\|\to 0$ (by Lemma~\ref{app:lem:null} below), so $d^{2}$ is of lower than quadratic order along such sequences.
\end{remark}

\subsection*{A.3\quad The null--sign equivalence}

\begin{lemma}[Null--sign equivalence]\label{app:lem:null}
Assume Axiom~E. If $\delta x_{n}\to 0$ with $\delta t_{n}>0$, $d(x;\delta x_{n})/\|\delta x_{n}\|\to 0$, and a subsequence of $v_{n}:=\delta x_{n}/\|\delta x_{n}\|$ converges to $v\in S^{1}$, then $Q(v)\le 0$. Conversely, if $Q(v)\le 0$ and $\delta x_{n}\to 0$ with $\delta t_{n}>0$ and $\delta x_{n}/\|\delta x_{n}\|\to v$, then $d(x;\delta x_{n})/\|\delta x_{n}\|\to 0$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
\textit{Forward:} By Axiom~E, $(d(x;\delta x_{n_{k}})/\|\delta x_{n_{k}}\|)^{2}=(Q(v_{n_{k}}))_{+}+o(1)\to 0$, so $(Q(v))_{+}=0$.

\textit{Converse:} Let $v_{n}:=\delta x_{n}/\|\delta x_{n}\|$, so $v_{n}\to v$. Since $Q$ is continuous and $Q(v)\le 0$, $(Q(v_{n}))_{+}\to 0$. By homogeneity,
\[
\bigl(Q(\delta x_{n})\bigr)_{+} = \|\delta x_{n}\|^{2}\bigl(Q(v_{n})\bigr)_{+} = o\!\left(\|\delta x_{n}\|^{2}\right).
\]
Hence Axiom~E gives $d(x;\delta x_{n})^{2}=o(\|\delta x_{n}\|^{2})$, so $d(x;\delta x_{n})/\|\delta x_{n}\|\to 0$.\qed
\end{proof}

\subsection*{A.4\quad The signature theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Lorentzian signature from realizability]\label{app:thm:lorentz}
Assume Axioms~R, E, T, and that $Q$ is nondegenerate. Then $Q$ is indefinite of signature $(1,1)$, and an invertible linear change of variables brings $Q$ to the form
\[
Q = dt'^{2} - c_{\max}^{-2}\,dr'^{2}
\]
for some $c_{\max}>0$. In these diagonalised coordinates, the null set consists of exactly two directions $dr'/dt'=\pm c_{\max}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\textbf{Step~1 (Null direction from~R).} By Axiom~R, there exists a subsequence $v_{n_{k}}\to v\in S^{1}$ with $v_{t}>0$, $v_{r}\neq 0$. By Lemma~\ref{app:lem:null}, $Q(v)\le 0$.

\textbf{Step~2 (Temporal direction positive).} If $Q(1,0)\le 0$, then by homogeneity $Q(\Delta t,0)=\Delta t^{2}Q(1,0)$, hence $(Q(\Delta t,0))_{+}=0$. Axiom~E therefore gives $d(x;(\Delta t,0))^{2}=o(\Delta t^{2})$, so $d(x;(\Delta t,0))=o(\Delta t)$, contradicting Axiom~T. Therefore $Q(1,0)>0$.

\textbf{Step~3 (Null vector by IVT).} Define
\[
\gamma(s):=\frac{(1-s)(1,0)+s\,v}{\|(1-s)(1,0)+s\,v\|}, \qquad s\in[0,1].
\]
Since $v_{r}\neq 0$, the denominator is nonzero for all $s\in[0,1]$, so $\gamma$ is well-defined. Since $v_{t}>0$, the numerator has strictly positive $t$-component throughout, so $\gamma$ is a continuous path in $S^{1}\cap\{u_{t}>0\}$. Then $Q(\gamma(0))=Q(1,0)>0$ and $Q(\gamma(1))=Q(v)\le 0$, so by the intermediate value theorem there exists~$s^{*}$ with $Q(\gamma(s^{*}))=0$. Set $w:=\gamma(s^{*})$; then $w_{t}>0$. If $w_{r}=0$, then $w=(1,0)$ because $w\in S^{1}$ and $w_{t}>0$, which would imply $Q(w)=Q(1,0)>0$, contradicting $Q(w)=0$. Hence $w_{r}\neq 0$.

\textbf{Step~4 (Signature and diagonal form).} By Step~2, $Q$ takes a positive value; by Step~3, $Q$ vanishes on a nonzero vector. Since $Q$ is nondegenerate, a nondegenerate quadratic form on~$\mathbb{R}^{2}$ with a nonzero null vector cannot be definite; hence it is indefinite, and in two dimensions the signature is $(1,1)$. An invertible linear change of variables brings $Q$ to the form $Q = dt'^{2} - c_{\max}^{-2}\,dr'^{2}$. Explicitly, $dt':=\sqrt{a}\bigl(dt+(\kappa/a)\,dr\bigr)$, $dr':=dr$ puts $Q$ in the displayed form with $c_{\max}^{-2}=(\kappa^{2}-ab)/a$, which is positive because $ab-\kappa^{2}<0$ by indefiniteness and $a=Q(1,0)>0$ by Step~2.\qed
\end{proof}

\subsection*{A.5\quad The nondegeneracy hypothesis is genuinely independent}

\begin{remark}[Nondegeneracy cannot be derived from R, E, T]\label{app:rem:nondeg}
Theorem~\ref{app:thm:lorentz} takes nondegeneracy of~$Q$ as an explicit hypothesis. Ruling out degeneracy from Axioms~R, E, T alone is not possible. Consider
\[
Q=(dt-\lambda\,dr)^{2}, \qquad \lambda\neq 0,
\]
realised by the cost function $d(x;\delta x):=\sqrt{(Q(\delta x))_{+}}$. Axiom~E holds with this $Q$ as an exact identity (no $o$-term needed). Axiom~T holds because $Q(e_{t})=1$ gives $d(x;(\Delta t,0))=\Delta t$. Axiom~R holds because the kernel of $Q$ is spanned by $u=(\lambda,1)$ (with the sign chosen so that the $t$-component is positive). Setting $\delta x_{n}:=\varepsilon_{n}u$ with $\varepsilon_{n}\downarrow 0$, one has $\delta x_{n}\to 0$, $\delta x_{n}/\|\delta x_{n}\|\to u/\|u\|$ (a direction with positive $t$-component), and $Q(\delta x_{n})=\varepsilon_{n}^{2}Q(u)=0$, so $d(x;\delta x_{n})/\|\delta x_{n}\|=0\to 0$, realising Axiom~R.

A tempting argument would be that degeneracy together with $Q(e_{t})>0$ should imply, in suitable coordinates, $Q=a\,dt^{2}$, forcing any null vector to have vanishing $t$-component. This is not valid: the reduction $Q=a\,dt^{2}$ holds only when the kernel vector has vanishing $t$-component, but the kernel $(\lambda,1)$ has $t$-component $\lambda\neq 0$. Nondegeneracy is therefore a genuinely independent condition, not derivable from R, E, T.
\end{remark}

\subsection*{A.6\quad Exclusion of non-Lorentzian signatures}

\begin{remark}[Why all non-Lorentzian signatures are excluded]\label{app:rem:exclusion}
Under the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{app:thm:lorentz}, every non-Lorentzian signature is excluded:
\begin{itemize}
\item[(i)] Positive-definite signature $(2,0)$ is excluded by Axiom~R together with Lemma~\ref{app:lem:null}: Axiom~R yields a nonzero limiting direction~$v$ with $v_{t}>0$ and $Q(v)\le 0$, which is impossible for a positive-definite form.
\item[(ii)] Negative-definite signature $(0,2)$ is excluded by Axiom~T: negative-definiteness gives $Q(1,0)<0$, hence $(Q(\Delta t,0))_{+}=0$ for all sufficiently small $\Delta t>0$; Axiom~E therefore yields $d(x;(\Delta t,0))=o(\Delta t)$, contradicting Axiom~T.
\item[(iii)] Degenerate forms are excluded by the explicit nondegeneracy hypothesis.
\end{itemize}
Lorentzian signature is therefore not merely compatible with the cost asymmetry; within the class defined by Axioms~R, E, T, and nondegeneracy in two dimensions, it is the unique outcome.
\end{remark}

\subsection*{A.7\quad Use in the main text}

In the body of the paper, the leading quadratic form $G$ of Law~I (\S\ref{sec:lawI}) is identified with the form $Q$ of this appendix. Axioms~R, E, T are imposed on the local cost-form layer of the information-time construction $dt_{\mathrm{info}}:=d\Phi/H$, and Theorem~\ref{app:thm:lorentz} then forces $\mathrm{Sig}(G)=(1,1)$, equivalently $\det G<0$. This is the operative bridge from cost-realizability to Lorentzian signature used throughout the framework; in particular, it supplies the input to Theorems~\ref{thm:spectral}--\ref{thm:equivalence}. The $(1{+}3)$-dimensional extension is not used in the main text and is therefore omitted from this appendix; the framework's higher-dimensional structure is discussed heuristically in \S\ref{sec:discussion}.

framework offers \emph{two} natural prescriptions for fixing it that
\emph{disagree}. The unresolved disagreement, not the correction term,
is the substantive content of this appendix.

\subsection*{B.1\quad The relaxed action and its indicial structure}

Replace the hard constraints by the $\eta$-weighted quadratic relaxation
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Seta}
S_\eta=\frac12\int\bigl[\eta\,(K_1-1)^2+(K_2-1)^2\bigr]\,r^2\,dr ,
\qquad \eta>0,
\end{equation}
with $K_1,K_2$ as in Definition~\ref{def:esc}. The two functionals are
not independent: a direct computation from Definition~\ref{def:esc}
gives the exact identity
\begin{equation}\label{eq:constraint}
K_1=K_2+\frac{r}{2}\,K_2',
\end{equation}
so the variational problem involves a \emph{single} field. Writing
$w:=K_2-1$, the homogeneous Euler--Lagrange equation of \eqref{eq:Seta}
is of Euler (equidimensional) type, with indicial relation
\begin{equation}\label{eq:indicial}
\lambda^2+3\lambda+2-\frac{4}{\eta}=0 ,
\qquad
\lambda_\pm=\frac{-3\pm\sqrt{1+16/\eta}}{2}.
\end{equation}
The trivial solution $w\equiv0$ ($K_2\equiv1$, the exact Schwarzschild
branch) solves the homogeneous equation for \emph{every} $\eta$, so the
mere existence of a vacuum branch cannot single out a weight.

\subsection*{B.2\quad The conditional $1/r^3$ correction}

Imposing the marginal-mode criterion $\lambda_+=0$ selects
\begin{equation}\label{eq:eta2}
\eta=2,\qquad \lambda\in\{0,-3\},
\end{equation}
and the general homogeneous solution then acquires a constant mode
beside the $r^{-3}$ mode, $w=w_0+\alpha/r^2+\beta/r^3$. Asymptotic
flatness removes $w_0$, and integrating $K_2=1+w$ gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fform}
\lambda_+=0\;\Longrightarrow\;
f(r)=1+\frac{C}{r}-\frac{\alpha}{r^2}-\frac{\beta}{2r^3}.
\end{equation}
Here $C=-2M$ is the Schwarzschild mass term and $\alpha=-Q^2$ is a
Reissner--Nordstr\"om-like integration mode (opposite in sign to the RN
charge term); the $\beta/r^3$ term is a non-GR signature absent from
both Schwarzschild and Reissner--Nordstr\"om. We stress that this is a
\emph{conditional} statement: it holds only given the criterion
$\lambda_+=0$, which is examined --- and found wanting --- next.

\subsection*{B.3\quad The unresolved weight ambiguity}

The status of the entire extension hinges on whether the weight $\eta$
can be fixed by the framework rather than imposed. It is here that the
framework, in its present form, fails to decide. Two natural
prescriptions arise, and they give \emph{different} weights.

\medskip\noindent\textit{(i) Dynamical (Euler--Lagrange) reading.}
The point-level null flow (\S\ref{sec:nullflow}) has a single conserved
charge, whose lift is the constant field mode $w=\mathrm{const}$.
Requiring this mode to be an admissible homogeneous Euler--Lagrange
solution of \eqref{eq:Seta} forces the coefficient $1-\eta/2$ to vanish:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:etaEL}
\text{Euler--Lagrange marginal mode}\;\Longrightarrow\;\eta=2 .
\end{equation}

\medskip\noindent\textit{(ii) Cost-theoretic (Axiom~R) reading.}
Axiom~R, at the point level, asserts the existence of a nonzero
\emph{zero-cost} direction; its honest field-level analogue demands a
nonzero field mode of vanishing cost density. A positive-definite
penalty admits no such mode, so realising Axiom~R requires the field
cost form to be Lorentzian (indefinite), mirroring the point-level
signature. Imposing that the constant direction be null for the
inherited indefinite form forces instead the coefficient $\eta-1$ to
vanish:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:etaR}
\text{Axiom-R zero-cost mode}\;\Longrightarrow\;\eta=1 .
\end{equation}

\medskip\noindent
\fbox{\parbox{0.95\linewidth}{\centering
The dynamical and cost-theoretic readings select
$\eta=2$ and $\eta=1$ respectively.\\[2pt]
\textbf{The framework currently does not resolve this tension.}}}

\medskip\noindent
The two prescriptions are not minor variants of one another: $\eta=2$
gives the clean integer-power solution \eqref{eq:fform}, whereas $\eta=1$
is the symmetric weight, with irrational indicial roots
$\lambda_\pm=(-3\pm\sqrt{17})/2$ from \eqref{eq:indicial} and no
integer-power correction at all. The choice between them is therefore
not a matter of normalisation but determines whether a clean $1/r^3$
signature exists. Because the framework supplies no principle selecting
\eqref{eq:etaEL} over \eqref{eq:etaR}, the weight $\eta$ --- and with it
the $\beta/r^3$ correction of \eqref{eq:fform} --- remains conditional on
an external choice and is not a theorem of the present $K{=}1$ framework.
The integration measure ($r^2\,dr$, the coordinate-area measure) is
likewise an input. Resolving the $\eta=1$ versus $\eta=2$ ambiguity is
the decisive logical node for this extension and is recorded as the
sharpened form of Open Problem~1.
